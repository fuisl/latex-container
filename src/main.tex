\begin{abstract}
Traffic signal control~(TSC) in urban networks requires coordinated decisions
across hundreds of intersections, a challenge well-addressed by multi-agent
reinforcement learning~(MARL). Agent communication is central to coordination
quality, yet existing MARL-TSC methods determine communication topology from
geometric proximity or learned reward signals---neither of which reflects the
underlying vehicle flow structure. We propose using the map
equation~(InfoMap)~\cite{rosvall2008maps} to partition road networks by
origin-destination~(OD) demand coherence, yielding communication topologies
in which within-module agent pairs have higher mutual information about future
traffic states. We operationalise this idea at three distinct levels: regional
reward shaping~(IDQN/IPPO), regional critic design~(MAPPO), and regional
communication graph~(CoLight-style regional GATs). A rigorous evaluation
protocol following the \emph{rliable}~\cite{agarwal2021rliable} methodology
specifies sample-efficiency metrics~(area under the learning curve, episodes to
threshold) with stratified bootstrap confidence intervals and performance
profiles. We further propose an attention range analysis---grounded in a
principled range measure for graph neural network operators~\cite{lrd2025}---to
mechanistically validate that InfoMap partitioning focuses attention within
flow-coherent modules. A synthesis of three converging bodies of
literature---MARL-TSC communication protocols, network partitioning for traffic
management, and flow-based community detection---reveals a clear gap: no
existing study applies flow-based community detection to define MARL
communication neighbourhoods in TSC. The information bottleneck~(IB) framework
widely applied in MARL communication~\cite{ding2021imac} provides motivating
grounding: InfoMap provides a domain-motivated communication prior that reduces
the topology search space that IB methods must otherwise explore from task
reward signals alone---though the formal equivalence between the two objectives
remains a conjecture requiring proof. We identify five research gaps, formalise
a testable hypothesis, and specify a complete multi-stage experimental design
for empirical validation.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

Urban traffic congestion imposes substantial economic and environmental costs.
Traffic signal control---the real-time management of intersection signal
phases---is among the most tractable levers available to transportation
engineers, yet optimal coordination across an urban network remains an open
problem. With $n$ signalised intersections, the joint action space grows
exponentially, making centralized optimal control computationally infeasible at
city scale.

Multi-agent reinforcement learning reframes the problem by assigning one agent
per intersection, each learning a local policy. Decentralisation makes the
approach scalable but introduces partial observability---each agent observes
only local traffic state---and the need for coordination. Communication between
agents is the primary mechanism for resolving partial observability, and the
structure of that communication (who communicates with whom, what is
communicated, and when) directly determines coordination quality.

The dominant approach to communication structure in MARL-TSC is geographic:
agents communicate with their $k$-hop road-network neighbours. This is
intuitively reasonable but ignores a critical property of traffic systems:
vehicle flow does not respect road-network adjacency. A vehicle entering
intersection $A$ may traverse several intermediate intersections before reaching
its true destination zone, making immediate neighbours of $A$ poor predictors
of its downstream impact.

We investigate whether partitioning the road network by vehicle flow
dynamics---specifically using the map equation (InfoMap)~\cite{rosvall2008maps}
---produces communication topologies that better capture the coordination
requirements of TSC. The map equation identifies communities as regions where a
random walker tends to stay; in traffic, the random walker is a vehicle and the
communities correspond to OD demand basins. The core hypothesis is that agents
within the same InfoMap module have higher mutual information about each
other's future traffic states, and therefore benefit more from communication
than pairs selected by geographic proximity.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item An information-theoretic argument connecting the map equation to
        MARL communication topology, formalised as testable Hypothesis~H1.
  \item A comprehensive literature synthesis across three bodies of work,
        identifying five concrete research gaps.
  \item A three-level experimental protocol operationalising the InfoMap
        partition at the reward level~(IDQN/IPPO), critic level~(MAPPO),
        and communication graph level~(regional GATs), with a factorial
        ablation design to isolate each level's contribution.
  \item A rigorous evaluation methodology based on the rliable
        framework~\cite{agarwal2021rliable}---interquartile mean~(IQM),
        performance profiles, and stratified bootstrap confidence
        intervals---addressing reproducibility concerns in MARL-TSC evaluation.
  \item An attention range analysis protocol---grounded in the range measure for
        graph neural network operators~\cite{lrd2025}---to mechanistically
        validate that regional GAT partitioning sharpens attention focus
        within flow-coherent modules.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background: The Map Equation and InfoMap}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================

The map equation~\cite{rosvall2008maps,rosvall2009mapequation} provides the
theoretical foundation. The core insight is that community structure in a
network can be identified by finding the partition that best compresses the
description of a random walk using the minimum description length~(MDL)
principle:

\begin{equation}
  L(\mathcal{M}) = q_{\curvearrowright} H(\mathcal{Q})
    + \sum_{i=1}^{k} p_i^{\circlearrowleft} H(\mathcal{P}_i)
  \label{eq:mapequation}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $q_{\curvearrowright}$ is the rate at which a random walker
moves \emph{between} modules, $H(\mathcal{Q})$ is the entropy of
between-module movement, $p_i^{\circlearrowleft}$ is the rate of staying
\emph{within} module $i$, and $H(\mathcal{P}_i)$ is the entropy of
within-module movement. Minimising $L(\mathcal{M})$ over all partitions
$\mathcal{M}$ identifies communities where the walker---and in traffic,
vehicles---tend to remain.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Key algorithmic properties}~\cite{edler2023community}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Handles directed, weighted edges without discarding direction (critical
        for traffic OD matrices);
  \item Does not require specifying the number of communities $k$ in advance;
  \item Does not suffer from the resolution limit affecting modularity-based
        methods (Louvain, Girvan-Newman);
  \item Produces a hierarchical codebook---a natural two-level communication
        vocabulary.
\end{itemize}

Applied to a traffic network, nodes are intersections and edge weights
represent directed OD flow between them. The partition that minimises
Eq.~\eqref{eq:mapequation} recovers intersections that vehicles tend to visit
together---OD-coherent demand basins.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope of the random walk approximation.}
The map equation assumes a memoryless random walk; real vehicle routing is
destination-directed and influenced by GPS and habitual paths. Applied to
aggregated intersection-pair flow counts from simulation or historical data,
the partition approximates OD demand basins rather than strict random walk
communities. This is a purposeful approximation: we use flow volume as a proxy
for flow coherence, and whether this is sufficient for defining effective MARL
communication topologies is precisely what Hypothesis~H1
(Section~\ref{sec:theory}) is designed to test empirically.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\subsection{MARL-TSC Communication Protocols}

\textbf{CoLight}~\cite{wei2019colight} applied graph attentional networks to
MARL-TSC, learning attention weights over the road-network graph to selectively
weight messages from neighbours. The key limitation is that the communication
graph \emph{equals} the road graph---no flow-based structure is incorporated.
Our proposed modification partitions the single global GAT into multiple
regional GATs aligned with InfoMap modules, replacing network-wide attention
with flow-coherent local attention.

\textbf{Representational communication}~\cite{bokade2023representational} uses
learned compressed representations as messages for large-scale TSC, demonstrating
that the quality of \emph{what} is communicated directly limits coordination
at scale. \textbf{CVDMARL}~\cite{cvdmarl2024} combines value decomposition with
explicit inter-agent message passing, confirming that structured communication
outperforms both independent learning and unstructured full communication.

\textbf{HiLight}~\cite{zhu2026hilight} uses a hierarchical policy that partitions
the network into subregions with a Transformer-LSTM meta-policy and generates
sub-goals for individual agents. Region definition is shown to affect
coordination quality---but the partition criterion is geographic or density-based,
not flow-based. \textbf{Li et al.}~\cite{li2025topology} integrate Dynamic GNNs
with Topological Data Analysis, demonstrating that explicit topological structure
beyond the road graph improves MARL representation quality.

\textbf{RegionLight}~\cite{regionlight2024} partitions the network into
geographic regions for hierarchical MARL-TSC, confirming that neighbourhood
definition shapes coordination quality.
\textbf{NeurComm}~\cite{neurcomm} enriches agent observations with
spatially discounted $k$-hop neighbour data through a fixed road-graph topology
with distance-weighted message passing. Both methods confirm that
neighbourhood definition matters, yet neither uses OD flow dynamics as
its partitioning signal.

\subsection{Communication Topology Design in MARL}

\textbf{ATOC}~\cite{jiang2018atoc} introduced attentional selective communication:
agents decide dynamically whether to initiate communication based on local
observations. This established that not all communication adds value.
\textbf{I2C}~\cite{i2c2021} learns per-pair communication value, achieving
powerful topology adaptation but without scalability or domain-structure grounding.
\textbf{Learning correlated topology}~\cite{aamas2021correlated} optimises the
communication graph as a latent variable, yielding dynamic topologies that are
opaque and non-transferable.

\textbf{ExpoComm}~\cite{expocomm2025} takes a structural approach: it uses
exponential graph theory to pre-design communication topologies with small
diameter and near-linear cost, reaching any agent in $O(\log n)$ hops. This
proves that pre-designed domain-grounded topologies can match or exceed learned
topologies at scale---directly motivating the InfoMap approach.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Domain prior vs.\ end-to-end learned topology.}
Methods such as ATOC and I2C learn \emph{who} to communicate with directly
from the task reward signal, and in principle sufficient training should
converge to a topology at least as good as any pre-specified alternative.
Three properties of the TSC setting argue for a domain prior:
(i)~\textbf{Sample efficiency} --- urban traffic networks have stable
demand structure, making an OD-based prior informative from the first episode
and reducing the topology exploration burden during training;
(ii)~\textbf{Training-time non-stationarity} --- topology learned under an
early random policy may differ from the optimal topology for the converged
policy, creating drift that a fixed domain prior avoids;
(iii)~\textbf{Zero-shot transfer} --- a task-reward-learned topology is
network-specific and must be relearned on each new deployment, whereas
InfoMap is recomputable from OD data alone.
These arguments are contingent on H1 holding in simulation; we treat them as
motivating conjectures, not established claims.

\textbf{Information Bottleneck approaches} formalise the communication quality
problem. IMAC~\cite{ding2021imac} compresses messages to retain only
task-relevant mutual information, achieving $>80\%$ message reduction without
performance loss. IB-VQ~\cite{sun2026ibvq} extends this to bandwidth-efficient
discrete coding. Heterogeneous IB Coordination Graphs~\cite{hetib2025} co-design
graph structure and per-edge bandwidth. The IB literature establishes the formal
question behind our hypothesis: what communication structure maximises
task-relevant mutual information while minimising message complexity?

\subsection{Network Partitioning for Traffic Signal Control}

\textbf{Laplacian eigenvector partitioning}~\cite{laplacian2009ieee} showed that
spectral methods can automatically identify signal coordination groups, but the
criterion is road-network topology rather than vehicle flow.

\textbf{OD-flow-based partitioning}~\cite{odflow2019partition} showed that
partitions derived from OD flow between intersections outperform conventional
adjacency-based methods on average delay and average speed---the strongest
empirical precedent for our hypothesis in classical TSC.

\textbf{MFD-based partitioning} applies community detection to identify
homogeneous traffic density regions for perimeter control~\cite{mfdpartition2020,%
communitydetection2021plosone}. The partition goal is density homogeneity, not
OD flow coherence.

\textbf{Feudal MARL with Adaptive Network Partition}~\cite{ma2022feudal} is
the closest prior work. It partitions the traffic network using GNN+MCTS to
find the optimal partition for a feudal policy hierarchy. Three distinctions
from our proposed approach: (i)~partition criterion is reward-driven, not
flow-based OD; (ii)~partition defines a policy hierarchy, not a communication
topology; (iii)~information-theoretic properties of the partition are not
analysed.

\textbf{GPLight}~\cite{liu2023gplight} groups agents by mutual information
over observation similarity using two differentiable loss functions. Three
distinctions: (i)~signal source is current observation (not future-state flow
dynamics); (ii)~purpose is parameter sharing, not communication topology
design; (iii)~clusters are learned during training whereas InfoMap is
pre-computed from domain data.

\subsection{InfoMap in Urban Mobility}

\textbf{Lucchini et al.}~\cite{lucchini2023intraod} apply InfoMap to
intra-urban OD networks and find that the core community structure is
temporally stable across time windows, while peripheral nodes show more
variation. This supports using a time-aggregated OD partition as a valid
first approximation, with boundary intersections requiring special handling.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Critical gap.} No existing study applies the InfoMap
codebook to specify \emph{MARL communication neighbourhoods} in TSC.

\subsection{Rigorous Evaluation in Deep Reinforcement Learning}

A recurring concern in both deep RL and MARL-TSC is evaluation reliability.
Henderson et al.~\cite{henderson2018matters} demonstrated that reported
performance differences often disappear with different random seeds,
hyperparameter choices, or evaluation protocols. For MARL-TSC specifically,
many published results rest on single-seed runs compared on final-episode ATT,
making it difficult to distinguish genuine algorithmic gains from variance.

Agarwal et al.~\cite{agarwal2021rliable} formalise a statistically sound
evaluation framework---\emph{rliable}---based on: (i)~\textbf{interquartile
mean~(IQM)}, which is more statistically efficient than median across $\leq 50$
runs while being robust to outliers; (ii)~\textbf{performance profiles}, which
report the full distribution of outcomes rather than a single scalar;
(iii)~\textbf{stratified bootstrap confidence intervals} across seeds and
environments; and (iv)~\textbf{probability of improvement}, which measures
how often algorithm $A$ outperforms algorithm $B$ across random draws.
Our experimental protocol adopts all four measures---a departure from
standard MARL-TSC practice---to ensure that sample-efficiency claims about
InfoMap partitioning are statistically defensible.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Information-Theoretic Hypothesis}

Let $X_i(t)$ denote the traffic state at intersection $i$ at time $t$.
Let $\mathcal{M} = \{M_1, M_2, \ldots, M_k\}$ be an InfoMap partition.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Testable Hypothesis H1.} For intersections $i, j$ in the
same module $M_k$ and $l \notin M_k$:

\begin{equation}
  I\!\left(X_i(t);\, X_j(t+\tau)\right) >
  I\!\left(X_i(t);\, X_l(t+\tau)\right),
  \quad \text{on average over } \tau \in [1, T].
  \label{eq:h1}
\end{equation}

This is \emph{motivated by}, but not formally derived from, the map equation:
if the intersection-pair flow graph reasonably approximates the random walk
model, then vehicles tend to remain within $M_k$, and congestion at $i$
should propagate preferentially to other intersections within $M_k$.
\textbf{We treat H1 explicitly as an empirical hypothesis, not a derived
theorem.}
The hypothesis connects to the IB framework for MARL communication: the
task-relevant component of agent $i$'s message to agent $j$ is
$I(M_i;\, A_j \mid O_j)$. InfoMap provides a domain-motivated
\emph{initialisation} for the communication structure that IB methods
would otherwise need to discover from reward --- a conjecture formalised
by H1 and tested in the experimental protocol (Section~\ref{sec:experiment}).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{H1 verification protocol.} Before MARL training,
collect $\sim$1000 warm-up steps with a random or rule-based policy.
For all intersection pairs $(i,j)$, compute empirical
$\hat{I}(X_i(t); X_j(t+\tau))$ via the KSG estimator at
$\tau \in \{1, 5, 15, 30\}$ steps. Classify each pair as within-module
(same InfoMap module) or cross-module. Test H1 with a Wilcoxon signed-rank
test; visualise as a $|V|\times|V|$ MI heatmap with InfoMap module boundaries
overlaid. If H1 fails on warm-up data, halt and revise the hypothesis before
full training.

\subsection{The Codebook as Communication Vocabulary}

InfoMap produces a hierarchical two-level codebook~\cite{rosvall2011multilevel}
that maps naturally to a two-level communication protocol:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Module codewords} (Level 2): compact regional identity
        (which demand basin an agent belongs to).
  \item \textbf{Within-module codewords} (Level 1): agent position within
        its region.
\end{enumerate}

This induces a two-level message structure:
agents within the same module exchange full state vectors
(within-module codewords), while agents in different modules exchange only
module-level aggregate representations (module codewords)---reducing
cross-module communication bandwidth while preserving intra-module
coordination fidelity.

\section{Proposed Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
% ============================================================

\subsection{OD Flow Graph Construction}
\label{sec:odconstruction}

The InfoMap algorithm requires a directed weighted graph $G=(V,E,W)$ where
$W_{ij}$ is the vehicle flow volume from intersection $i$ to intersection $j$.
This is not a standard simulator output and requires explicit instrumentation.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{In CityFlow:} Enable vehicle trajectory logging via
the \texttt{roadnet.json} configuration. For each vehicle trip, extract the
ordered sequence of intersections traversed. For each consecutive pair
$(i, j)$ in the sequence, increment $W_{ij}$. After the warm-up period,
normalise by total vehicle count to obtain a directed flow-proportion matrix.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{In SUMO:} Use the \texttt{edgedata} output with
\texttt{tripinfo} to reconstruct intersection-pair traversal counts.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Cold-start problem.} The warm-up phase (first
$\sim$1000 steps) uses a random or rule-based policy before MARL training
begins. The OD flow under a random policy may differ from the OD flow under
the converged MARL policy, because signal timing affects which routes vehicles
take. Two mitigations:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Historical OD data} (preferred when available): use real-world
        OD matrices from the target city rather than simulation warm-up, decoupling
        partition quality from warm-up policy.
  \item \textbf{Iterative re-partitioning}: after $N$ training epochs, collect
        fresh OD data from the partially-trained policy and re-run InfoMap.
        Repeat once (re-partitioning more than twice risks training instability).
\end{enumerate}
This limitation is acknowledged; its severity is assessed via the ablation
comparing warm-up-OD partition vs.\ historical-OD partition.

\subsection{InfoMap Partitioning and H1 Verification}

Before MARL training begins, the road network is partitioned and H1 is
verified on warm-up data (Algorithm~\ref{alg:infomap}).

\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \caption{InfoMap Communication Topology Construction}
  \label{alg:infomap}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require Directed weighted graph $G=(V,E,W)$ (from Section~\ref{sec:odconstruction});
             $V$=intersections, $W_{ij}$=OD flow volume
    \Ensure Partition $\mathcal{M}=\{M_1,\ldots,M_k\}$; codebook $C$;
            inter-module adjacency matrix $A^{\text{cross}}$
    \State \textbf{(Cold-start)} If historical OD available, use as $W$;
           else collect $W$ via random-policy warm-up (flag policy mismatch)
    \State Run \textsc{Infomap}$(G)$ $\to$ $\mathcal{M}$, codebook $C$
    \State \textbf{Assert non-degeneracy:} $1 < k < |V|$ and all
           $|M_i| \geq 2$ (degenerate partition $\Rightarrow$ check OD data)
    \State Verify H1: compute $\hat{I}(X_i;X_j)$ for within- vs.\ cross-module
           pairs via KSG estimator; test with Wilcoxon signed-rank
    \State \textbf{if} H1 fails \textbf{then} halt and revise hypothesis
    \State Construct $A^{\text{cross}}$: inter-module edges with aggregate flow
    \State \Return $\mathcal{M}$, $C$, $A^{\text{cross}}$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Three-Level Application Protocol}
\label{sec:threelevels}

The InfoMap partition can be applied at three orthogonal levels within a
MARL-TSC system, each targeting a different inductive bias:

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Level 1 --- Regional reward shaping (IDQN / IPPO):}
Replace the single-intersection reward $r_i(t)$ with an intra-module average:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{r}_i(t) = \alpha\, r_i(t) + (1-\alpha)\,
  \frac{1}{|M_k|} \sum_{j \in M_k} r_j(t), \quad i \in M_k.
  \label{eq:regionalreward}
\end{equation}
This encourages agents within the same flow basin to internalise each other's
congestion signals without requiring any explicit communication channel.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Level 2 --- Regional critic design (MAPPO):}
Replace the global centralised critic $V(s)$ with $k$ regional critics,
each receiving only the joint state of agents within module $M_k$:
$V_k\!\left(\{o_i\}_{i \in M_k}\right)$.
This reduces the critic's input dimensionality from $O(n)$ to $O(n/k)$,
improving credit assignment within flow-coherent groups while preserving
the CTDE training paradigm.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Level 3 --- Regional communication graph (CoLight):}
Replace the single global Graph Attention Network with $k$ regional GATs,
one per InfoMap module. Each regional GAT computes attention only among
agents within its module. Cross-module coordination is handled by a lightweight
inter-module GAT operating on module-level aggregate representations.
This implements the two-level codebook structure from Section~\ref{sec:theory}
as a direct architectural change to the CoLight attention mechanism.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Composability.} The three levels are orthogonal:
Level~1 affects reward signals, Level~2 affects value estimation, and Level~3
affects message passing. They can be applied independently or in combination.
The factorial ablation design in Section~\ref{sec:factorial} isolates the
contribution of each.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experiment}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Simulation Environments}

\noindent\textbf{Platform:} CityFlow or SUMO with LibSignal benchmark
wrappers~\cite{libsignal}, enabling reproducible comparison against published
baselines.

\noindent\textbf{Networks:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Synthetic $6{\times}6$ grid (core experiment, all baselines);
  \item Synthetic $10{\times}10$ grid (scalability ablation);
  \item Real-world: Cologne8, Ingolstadt21 (RESCO standard benchmarks);
  \item Jinan $3{\times}4$, Manhattan segment (transferability test).
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Demand scenarios:}
uniform demand; AM/PM peak-hour OD patterns; mixed peak/off-peak.
Multiple demand scenarios on the same network produce different InfoMap
partitions (different $L(\mathcal{M})$ and intra-partition flow ratios
$\phi_k$), enabling regression analysis of which partition quality metrics
predict RL improvement (RQ3).

\noindent\textbf{Episode length:} 3600-second simulation with 15-second
phase intervals (CTDE framework: MAPPO or QMIX base algorithm).

\noindent\textbf{Seeds:} minimum 5 seeds per condition (defensible minimum);
8 seeds recommended; all seeds fixed and reported for reproducibility.

\subsection{Baseline Ladder and Sparsification Control}

Table~\ref{tab:baselines} defines the experimental conditions.
Baseline L1.5~(random sparse graph, same per-agent degree as the InfoMap
partition) is the critical control for the sparsification confound: if the
InfoMap condition (L4) beats L1.5, the benefit is attributable to
\emph{flow-coherent} topology selection, not merely to communication graph
sparsification. Without this control, any advantage of InfoMap over
$k$-hop neighbours is confounded between flow structure and reduced degree.

\subsection{Rigorous Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:evalmethod}

Standard MARL-TSC evaluation reports final-episode ATT averaged across a small
number of runs, which is insufficient for statistically credible comparisons
\cite{henderson2018matters}. We adopt the rliable framework
\cite{agarwal2021rliable} throughout.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Primary task metrics:} average travel time~(ATT,
seconds/vehicle; primary), average waiting time~(AWT), average queue
length~(AQL). ATT is the canonical comparison metric across RESCO and
LibSignal.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Sample efficiency metrics} (the central claim):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Area Under Learning Curve (AUC-LC):} $\int_0^N \text{ATT}(e)\,de$
        over $N$ training episodes via trapezoid rule. Lower AUC-LC means
        less cumulative delay incurred during training---directly measuring
        how quickly the agent learns to control traffic well.
  \item \textbf{Episodes to Threshold (ET-90, ET-95):} number of training
        episodes to reach 90\% and 95\% of the best observed ATT for that
        condition. ET-90 is more robust to final-episode noise; ET-95 provides
        a stricter benchmark.
  \item \textbf{Convergence plateau:} episode range where ATT standard
        deviation falls below 0.5\% of the mean, measuring training stability.
\end{itemize}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Aggregate statistics (rliable protocol):}
All aggregate metrics are reported as IQM $\pm$ 95\% stratified bootstrap CI
(2000 bootstrap samples, stratified across seeds and environments). Pairwise
\emph{probability of improvement} $\Pr(\text{InfoMap} > \text{METIS})$ is
reported with 95\% CI. Performance profiles plot the fraction of (algorithm,
environment, seed) triples achieving at least $x$\% of the best observed ATT,
for $x \in [0,1]$, providing a full distributional view.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Statistical tests:} Wilcoxon signed-rank test on
per-run AUC-LC scores for pairwise comparisons. Bonferroni correction applied
across the family of $k$ comparisons in the factorial design. Effect size
reported as rank-biserial correlation $r$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Partition quality regression (RQ3):} for each
network/demand scenario, compute intra-partition flow ratio $\phi_k$,
map equation value $L(\mathcal{M})$, graph conductance $\Phi$, and
NMI between InfoMap and METIS partitions. Regress $\Delta$AUC-LC
(InfoMap $-$ METIS) on these metrics to identify which partition property
predicts RL improvement. A significant positive coefficient on $\phi_k$
would support the claim that flow coherence, not merely graph structure,
drives the benefit.

\subsection{Attention Range Analysis}
\label{sec:attnrange}

For the CoLight-based conditions (regional GAT vs.\ global GAT), we conduct
a mechanistic analysis to validate \emph{why} partition benefits the
communication level.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Range measure for GNN operators.}
We apply the framework of~\cite{lrd2025}, which defines a principled
\emph{range measure} $\rho(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{A})$ quantifying how far
influence propagates through a graph attention operator $\mathcal{A}$ on
graph $\mathcal{G}$. For the trained CoLight GAT (global and regional), we
compute $\rho$ as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item For a target intersection $i$, compute the attention-weighted
        influence score $s_{ij}$ from all other intersections $j$ in the
        trained model.
  \item Plot $s_{ij}$ vs.\ graph distance $d(i,j)$ for within-module
        and cross-module pairs separately.
  \item Compute the effective attention radius $r^* = \arg\max_r \sum_{j: d(i,j) \leq r} s_{ij} \geq 0.9$.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Attention rollout visualisation.}
We additionally apply attention rollout~\cite{abnar2020rollout} to trace
cumulative influence through stacked attention layers backward from each
agent's decision, revealing which intersections' states actually drove
the action. Visualising these rollout heatmaps before and after partitioning
shows whether attention is ``leaking'' across module boundaries in the
global GAT but concentrated within modules in the regional GAT.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Expected finding.} Regional GATs should exhibit
a sharper attention falloff with distance (lower $\rho$, more concentrated
$s_{ij}$), with the cross-module GAT carrying appropriate long-range
aggregate signals. This would mechanistically confirm that InfoMap
partitioning aligns the GAT attention radius with the natural scale of
flow-coherent coordination---the specific mechanism by which H1 translates
into communication efficiency.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Anticipated Benefits}

\textbf{Coordination efficiency at multiple levels.} Within-module agents
are more predictive of each other's future states (H1), so each message
carries higher task-relevant mutual information. This translates into better
coordination per unit communication cost at the communication level~(L3).
At the critic level~(L2), regional critics reduce spurious credit assignment
from distant agents in different flow basins, accelerating MAPPO convergence.
At the reward level~(L1), the regional reward signal provides a denser
learning signal for agents whose individual reward is noisy but whose
module-level reward is informative.

\textbf{Sample efficiency.} The OD-based partition provides a strong inductive
bias from episode one, reducing the topology exploration burden. The primary
empirical claim---that InfoMap improves AUC-LC over METIS---is attributable
to flow coherence rather than graph sparsification if the random sparse
baseline~(L1.5) is also beaten.

\textbf{Scalability.} InfoMap module size grows sub-linearly with network
size: per-agent communication cost is $O(n/k)$ within modules rather than
$O(n)$, and regional critic input dimensionality is $O(n/k)$ rather than
$O(n)$.

\textbf{Interpretability.} Communities correspond to identifiable OD demand
basins that traffic engineers can validate independently of the MARL training
pipeline. The attention range analysis provides an additional mechanistic
visualisation layer for validation.

\textbf{Partition transferability.} The InfoMap partition is recomputable from
any OD flow matrix without MARL training, which is faster than re-running a full
topology learning procedure. The MARL policy still requires retraining on the
new network; partition transferability reduces only the \emph{topology design}
step, not the policy learning step. Zero-shot partition transfer is tested
in Section~\ref{sec:experiment} to bound this claim.

\subsection{Relationship to Prior Work}

The closest algorithmic prior, \textbf{Ma \& Wu}~\cite{ma2022feudal}, uses
reward-driven partitioning in a feudal policy hierarchy. Our contribution is
orthogonal: we use a flow-grounded, information-theoretically motivated
partition that targets communication topology, reward shaping, and critic
design rather than policy hierarchy, and we provide an explicit formal
connection to the IB framework.

\textbf{GPLight}~\cite{liu2023gplight} groups agents by observation MI---the
current-state signal. Our approach uses OD flow dynamics---the future-state
propagation signal---which is directly interpretable as traffic infrastructure
and transferable to new networks. The two criteria are complementary and
could be combined.

The rliable evaluation protocol~\cite{agarwal2021rliable} addresses the
reproducibility concern raised by Henderson et al.~\cite{henderson2018matters}
and is, to our knowledge, not yet standard practice in MARL-TSC evaluation.
Adopting it allows our results to be directly compared across methods
regardless of the number of seeds used in prior work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have presented a research proposal to define MARL communication
topologies for traffic signal control using the map equation (InfoMap).
The central hypothesis is that OD-flow-coherent communities induce agent
communication structures with higher task-relevant mutual information than
geographic or reward-driven alternatives. A synthesis of three literature
bodies reveals no prior work applying flow-based community detection to MARL
communication topology in TSC, and provides formal IB-theoretic grounding for
the approach.

Beyond the communication topology, the proposed framework operationalises the
InfoMap partition at three orthogonal levels---reward shaping, regional critics,
and regional GATs---with a factorial ablation design to isolate each level's
contribution. Rigorous sample-efficiency evaluation (AUC-LC, ET-90/ET-95, IQM
with stratified bootstrap CIs) ensures that the claimed convergence speed
advantage is statistically defensible. The attention range analysis provides
a mechanistic lens on \emph{why} the partition benefits communication: by
aligning the GAT's effective attention radius with the natural scale of
flow-coherent coordination.

The immediate next step is empirical validation of Hypothesis~H1 on warm-up
simulation data before full training---a two-hour experiment that either
confirms the theoretical foundation or forces hypothesis revision before
committing to full-scale training runs.
