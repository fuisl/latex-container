\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{ch:ResultsAndDiscussion}

This chapter presents and interprets the experimental results obtained from the FGS framework evaluated on German RESCO traffic scenarios. Performance figures are derived from Weights \& Biases experiment logs (project \texttt{marl-traffic-gat}). Scenarios follow the three-tier taxonomy established in Section~\ref{sec:Introduction_Scope}: \textbf{Single-intersection} (cologne1, ingolstadt1), \textbf{Corridor} (cologne3, ingolstadt7), and \textbf{Regional} (cologne8, ingolstadt21). Training ran for 200 episodes where the run completed; the first-generation FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC) run on ingolstadt21 terminated after 17 episodes due to resource exhaustion from the centralized critic's $O(N)$ input scaling at $N=21$. FGSv2 and FGSv3 variants address this bottleneck and complete 200 episodes at $N=21$.

\section{Main Results}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_MainResults}

\paragraph{Reporting conventions.}
All performance figures in this chapter are derived from the best-validation checkpoint observed during training — the policy snapshot that minimised mean validation waiting time on a fixed held-out demand seed. As noted in Section~\ref{sec:ExperimentalDesign_EvaluationSetup}, best-checkpoint figures represent an optimistic upper bound on deployed performance. Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Convergence} identifies cases where the reported best checkpoint is an early-training transient rather than a converged result; those entries are marked~$\ddagger$ in the tables. Additionally, FGS-variant results for the Ingolstadt Corridor (ingolstadt7, $N=7$) were not available within the available training budget and are excluded from the FGS comparison columns throughout this chapter; see Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Limitations} for context.

Several patterns are immediately visible. First, on the Cologne Regional scenario ($N=8$), PPO achieves the best mean waiting time at 4.3~s, with $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) and FGSv3-PPO close behind at 4.9~s and 4.8~s respectively, and DQN at 6.4~s. CoLight achieves 11.0~s and standalone FRAP 13.9~s — both substantially worse than the learning methods on this scenario. Second, on the Ingolstadt Regional scenario ($N=21$), standalone PPO now achieves 25.8~s mean wait, the best result among all methods on this network. DQN achieves 33.8~s. These updated values, derived from best-checkpoint evaluation of the best available run for each algorithm, are substantially better than older runs available at the time of earlier analysis. The first-generation $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ achieves 88.6~s on ingolstadt21 — worse than PPO and DQN — while FGSv3-PPO achieves 22.7~s (stable best-checkpoint), a 74\% reduction over $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$. Third, performance degrades sharply when the FRAP encoder is used: FGS(FRAP+GAT+SAC) produces dramatically worse results across all scenarios, with the Cologne Corridor showing a collapse to 460.8~s mean wait (versus 7.4~s for the MLP counterpart). Full results across all six scenarios and all variants are provided in Appendix~\ref{ch:Appendix_FullResults}, Tables~\ref{tab:appendix_baselines}--\ref{tab:appendix_throughput}.

\section{Baseline Comparison}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_BaselineComparison}

\subsection{FGS versus CoLight}

CoLight \citep{wei2019colight} is the most relevant prior graph-based MARL baseline, as it also employs inter-agent attention over the road topology. On the Cologne Regional scenario ($N=8$), CoLight achieves a mean waiting time of 11.0~s, compared to FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC)'s 4.9~s — a 55\% reduction. The gap can be attributed to three factors. First, CoLight uses dot-product attention with globally shared keys and queries, whereas GATv2 uses per-edge additive attention that captures asymmetric neighborhood relationships \citep{brody2022how}. Second, CoLight was designed for and validated on regular Chinese grid networks; the irregular topology of the German Corridor and Regional scenarios causes its attention mechanism to assign nearly uniform weights across heterogeneous neighbors. Third, CoLight does not employ a phase-aware local encoder, aggregating raw phase-density tensors directly — a representation that is noisy when phase counts vary from two to six across intersections, as in the German scenarios.

On the Ingolstadt Regional scenario ($N=21$), $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) achieves 88.6~s versus CoLight's 95.2~s — a 7\% improvement in mean waiting time. The third-generation FGSv3-PPO reduces this further to 22.7~s (best-checkpoint; see Table~\ref{tab:fgs_evolution}), outperforming CoLight by 76\%. On the Ingolstadt Corridor, CoLight achieves 17.5~s wait, worse than independent DQN (9.4~s) and PPO (14.7~s); FGS variants on the Ingolstadt Corridor are pending re-run. Taken together, GATv2 attention combined with a capable RL algorithm consistently outperforms the best published graph-based baseline on German scenarios at all tested scales.

\subsection{FGS versus independent DQN and PPO}

The independent DQN baseline represents a pure value-based approach without graph communication. On the Cologne Regional scenario ($N=8$), DQN achieves 6.4~s wait versus $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC)'s 4.9~s, confirming that graph communication and entropy-regularized learning provide a meaningful advantage on this network. On the Ingolstadt Corridor, DQN achieves 9.4~s wait — the best baseline result for that scenario — while PPO achieves 14.7~s. FGS variants have not yet been run on the Ingolstadt Corridor.

On the Ingolstadt Regional scenario ($N=21$), the updated best-available runs show DQN achieving 33.8~s and PPO achieving 25.8~s — both substantially better than earlier less-trained runs. The first-generation $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) achieves 88.6~s, worse than both DQN and PPO on this network. Two factors explain this gap: (1) DQN uses a simpler per-agent Q-network without graph inputs, enabling faster convergence within 200 episodes; (2) PPO's on-policy gradient updates converge reliably on large networks without the memory overhead of a centralized critic. The third-generation FGSv3-PPO reduces the gap, achieving 22.7~s (Table~\ref{tab:fgs_evolution}), which is competitive with standalone PPO (25.8~s) and better than DQN (33.8~s).

On the Cologne Corridor (cologne3), PPO achieves 6.0~s wait, DQN 8.1~s, and $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) 7.4~s. All three learning baselines converge to competitive solutions on this three-intersection network. FGS falls between PPO and DQN in absolute terms, indicating that at $N=3$ the graph communication layer does not confer a net advantage over the stronger single-agent baselines within the 200-episode training budget.

An independent single-agent SAC baseline (IndSAC), run without graph communication or centralized critic, achieves 5.5~s on the Cologne Corridor and 4.7~s on the Cologne Regional scenario. This result establishes that the entropy-regularized SAC objective itself is a strong baseline on small-to-moderate German scenarios, independent of graph aggregation. Full IndSAC results are tabulated in Appendix~\ref{ch:Appendix_FullResults}, Table~\ref{tab:appendix_baselines}.

\subsection{FGS versus standalone FRAP}

The standalone FRAP encoder \citep{zheng2019learning}, used as a single-agent controller without graph communication or RL, achieves 18.6~s on cologne3, 13.9~s on cologne8, and 51.0~s on ingolstadt21. These results are considerably worse than the corresponding $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$/FGSv3-PPO results (4.9/4.8~s on cologne8; 88.6/22.7~s on ingolstadt21) and worse than CoLight on cologne8 (11.0~s). The standalone FRAP result on ingolstadt21 (51.0~s) is notably better than the first-generation $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ (88.6~s), confirming that at $N=21$ the combination of MLP encoder, GATv2, and PPO within the first-generation architecture does not yet yield reliable coordination gains. These results also establish that FRAP's phase-competition prior, in isolation, does not provide competitive performance on German intersection geometries — yet when FRAP encoder outputs are used in FGSv3 with the demand-derived signal pathway (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv3}), the architectural mismatch is substantially reduced.

The convergence trajectories reveal qualitatively distinct dynamics across RL algorithms and network scales.

\paragraph{FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC): Cologne Regional.}
This run achieves stable convergence on cologne8. The validation waiting time exhibits large variance in early training (episodes~1--13: 438--660~s) as SAC's entropy-driven exploration produces widely varying phase schedules. From episode~14 onward, the policy improves monotonically, stabilizing near 6--7~s between episodes~22 and~35, with occasional brief spikes. The best validation checkpoint (6.0~s at episode~39) lies within the stable region, confirming this is a genuine converged result rather than a transient low.

\paragraph{FGSv3-PPO: Cologne Regional.}
FGSv3-PPO is the most stable and fastest-converging run on cologne8 across all FGS variants. Validation performance reaches 5.4~s by episode~5 and remains in the narrow band 4.8--6.1~s for the remainder of training (episodes~5--40). This stability reflects PPO's on-policy gradient updates, which avoid the large early-training excursions characteristic of off-policy SAC exploration. The best checkpoint (4.8~s at episode~39) falls within the converged band.

\paragraph{FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC): Ingolstadt Regional.}
The first-generation SAC variant on the twenty-one-intersection network recorded only 17 training episodes before process termination. The waiting time trace during these 17 episodes showed values fluctuating between 75~s and 185~s, with no improvement trend. This behavior is consistent with the centralized critic's input dimensionality at $N=21$: the flattened graph context vector and joint action representation fed to the twin Q-networks has approximately 3,133 dimensions (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv2}), requiring substantially more GPU memory and compute per gradient step than the PPO value function. The final checkpoint evaluated on the validation seed produced a mean waiting time of 757.5~s, indicating that partial training left the policy in a highly suboptimal state.

\paragraph{FGS\textsubscript{P}(MLP+GATv2+PPO): Ingolstadt Regional.}
The first-generation PPO counterpart completed all 200 training episodes. The training waiting time drops from approximately 58~s at episode~11, stabilizing in the range 29--34~s between episodes~140 and~200. The curve contains periodic spikes (200--340~s at isolated episodes) followed by rapid recovery, indicating that GATv2 attention propagates demand-spike signals across the graph within two to three episodes. The best validation performance (88.6~s) reflects a substantial train-to-validation gap (29~s training vs.\ 88.6~s validation), suggesting the policy is optimized for the training demand distribution.

\paragraph{FGSv3-PPO: Ingolstadt Regional.}
FGSv3-PPO is the best-performing FGS variant on ingolstadt21 with a stable convergence profile. The validation curve stabilizes in the 22--35~s range from episode~7 onward (median 32~s), with the best checkpoint at 22.7~s (episode~17). Unlike $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$, the factored critic in FGSv3 (input $O(d_c + A_\text{max}) = 204$~dims vs.\ 3,091 for FGS\textsubscript{S}) enables efficient gradient updates, contributing to faster stabilization.

\paragraph{FGSv3-SAC and FGSv2-SAC: Ingolstadt Regional.}
Both SAC variants complete training on ingolstadt21 thanks to the factored critic, but exhibit high-variance trajectories. FGSv3-SAC achieves its best validation checkpoint (11.1~s) at validation episode~3 — early in training when SAC's entropy bonus drives broad exploration, and the evaluation seed draws a particularly favorable traffic realization. All subsequent checkpoints settle in the 17--74~s range (median~25.8~s after episode~6). FGSv2-SAC shows similar behavior (best 13.9~s at episode~1; subsequent median~27.8~s). These patterns indicate that 100 training episodes are insufficient for SAC to converge on a 21-intersection network; the reported best-checkpoint values for these SAC variants should be interpreted as lower bounds on achievable performance with extended training, not as evidence of reliable policy quality.

\paragraph{DQN: Ingolstadt Regional.}
The DQN training trace shows a distinct pattern: the waiting time begins near 48~s and initially exhibits large spikes, but stabilizes around 33--50~s by episode~140. The best validation performance (33.8~s) reflects a stable but not well-coordinated policy — each agent optimizes its own Q-value without awareness of upstream or downstream implications. DQN's relatively strong result compared to first-generation FGS variants reflects the algorithm's simplicity: fewer trainable parameters and no graph communication overhead means all 200 episodes are used efficiently for value function learning.

\paragraph{FGS(MLP+GAT+PPO): Ingolstadt Regional.}
Replacing GATv2 with the original dot-product GAT communication produces markedly worse convergence on ingolstadt21. The training curve shows frequent and severe spikes throughout all 200 episodes, with the mean waiting time oscillating between 37~s and 330~s. The best observed validation performance (261.2~s) versus the GATv2 variant (88.6~s) reflects GAT's documented tendency to assign static attention coefficients that cannot adapt to unseen topology-demand combinations \citep{brody2022how}.

\paragraph{Effect of encoder type.}
The encoder ablation on Cologne Regional shows that replacing FRAP with MLP reduces mean waiting time from 87.6~s to 5.5~s when paired with GAT (a 16$\times$ improvement), and from 6.7~s to 4.9~s when paired with GATv2 (a modest 27\% improvement). The large FRAP penalty with GAT and the modest FRAP penalty with GATv2 suggest a partial interaction: GATv2's stronger attention mechanism partially compensates for FRAP's misaligned phase-competition features by re-weighting the most informative neighborhood components, whereas the weaker GAT attention propagates FRAP's noise directly. However, even with GATv2, FRAP+GATv2+PPO (6.7~s) underperforms MLP+GATv2+SAC (4.9~s), confirming that FRAP does not provide net benefit on German scenarios. On Ingolstadt Regional, FRAP+GATv2+PPO (460.7~s) is dramatically worse than MLP+GATv2+PPO (88.6~s), confirming that FRAP's encoding failure amplifies at larger scale.

\paragraph{Effect of graph communication layer.}
The most striking result in the ablation is the effect of substituting GATv2 for the plain GAT layer on the Ingolstadt Regional scenario: mean waiting time drops from 261.2~s to 88.6~s (a 66\% reduction). This large margin is consistent with GATv2's theoretical expressiveness advantage: the per-edge dynamic attention mechanism of GATv2 can distinguish structurally similar nodes by their feature content, whereas dot-product GAT assigns the same attention pattern to nodes with the same features regardless of their neighbors \citep{brody2022how}. In the Ingolstadt Regional's irregular topology, where each intersection has a unique combination of upstream and downstream demand profiles, this dynamic distinguishability is critical for learning useful neighborhood aggregations. The difference is negligible on Cologne Regional (4.9~s vs.\ 6.3~s), consistent with the expectation that in smaller, more homogeneous networks the two attention mechanisms converge to similar solutions.

\paragraph{Effect of RL algorithm.}
On Cologne Regional, SAC outperforms PPO with the same encoder and graph layer: 4.9~s versus 6.2~s, a 21\% improvement. This is consistent with SAC's off-policy sample efficiency advantage on moderate networks: the PER replay buffer exploits past transitions more efficiently than PPO's on-policy rollouts of equivalent length. On Ingolstadt Regional, the result reverses dramatically — PPO completes 200 training episodes while first-generation SAC terminates after 17, resulting in PPO achieving 88.6~s versus SAC's 757.5~s. The difference arises from the centralized critic's input scaling: at $N=21$ the critic input dimensionality of approximately 3,133 exceeds the resource capacity of the training node, while PPO's per-agent value function input remains constant at the local observation dimension regardless of network size. FGSv3 addresses this by replacing the centralized critic with a factored neighborhood critic ($O(d_c + A_\text{max}) = 204$~dims), enabling both SAC and PPO to complete training at $N=21$.

\section{Interpretation and Discussion}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Interpretation}

\subsection{FRAP encoding and German intersection geometry}

The consistent underperformance of FRAP-based variants across all scenarios — with mean waiting time increases ranging from $3\times$ (ingolstadt21 with GATv2+PPO) to $62\times$ (cologne3 with GAT+SAC) relative to MLP counterparts — warrants a structural explanation. FRAP \citep{zheng2019learning} constructs phase-pair competition features $\mathbf{c}_{i,a,b}$ by comparing the movement embedding of phase $a$ against each competing phase $b$, under the assumption that each phase controls a fixed pair of opposing movements. German RESCO intersections, however, have heterogeneous phase structures: cologne8 and ingolstadt21 mix junctions with 2, 4, and 6 phases, each controlling a variable number of movements. When a phase controls fewer than two movements, the competition weight normalization $\min(\sum P, 2) / \max(\sum P, 1)$ in FRAP's phase-pair embedding falls below the nominal value, producing undersaturated features that the downstream SAC actor misinterprets as low-demand signals. In the extreme case where a phase controls a single pedestrian phase or a turn-only movement, the FRAP output is near-zero — not because demand is low, but because the encoding is structurally inapplicable. This structural mismatch explains the catastrophic outcomes: the actor receives misleading phase utilities and selects phases that satisfy FRAP's encoding assumptions rather than the actual traffic demand.

The exception — FGS(FRAP+GATv2+PPO) on cologne8 achieving competitive 6.7~s wait — suggests that GATv2's attention can partially correct for FRAP's misaligned features by down-weighting neighbors whose FRAP outputs are near-zero (indicating encoding failure rather than low demand). This self-correcting property is not available in the weaker GAT variant, explaining the asymmetric performance gap between FRAP+GAT (87.6~s) and FRAP+GATv2+PPO (6.7~s) on cologne8. FGSv3 addresses the root cause by introducing a demand-branch communication pathway that bypasses the FRAP phase-competition step (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv3}), making inter-agent signals robust to phase structure heterogeneity.

\subsection{Graph-based coordination and network scale}

The results support a nuanced view of H1: graph-based communication provides measurable benefit, but the benefit depends critically on both network scale and the generation of the FGS architecture. The ablation shows that GATv2 provides a 66\% improvement over GAT on Ingolstadt Regional (88.6 vs.\ 261.2~s), while the improvement on Cologne Regional is modest (4.9 vs.\ 5.5~s, an 11\% reduction — MLP+GATv2+SAC vs.\ MLP+GAT+SAC). Comparing $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) to CoLight on ingolstadt21 (88.6~s vs.\ 95.2~s) demonstrates that GATv2's dynamic attention outperforms CoLight's static neighborhood representation by 7\%. The benefit is substantially clearer with FGSv3-PPO (22.7~s vs.\ CoLight 95.2~s), a 76\% improvement, because the factored critic eliminates the computational bottleneck that limited first-generation FGS training depth.

The comparison of $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) against the independent baselines on Ingolstadt Regional reveals an important nuance in first-generation FGS: graph communication does not automatically overcome the convergence gap between architectures. DQN (33.8~s) and PPO (25.8~s) substantially outperform $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ (88.6~s) despite lacking inter-agent communication. The training curves reveal that $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ achieves a best training waiting time of $\approx$29~s by episode~190 — better than DQN's training optimum of $\approx$38~s — but the train-to-validation gap for $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ (29~s train vs.\ 88.6~s validation) is larger than for DQN (38~s train vs.\ 33.8~s validation). This suggests that the GATv2-PPO combination in the first-generation architecture develops a more demand-specific but less generalizable policy. FGSv3-PPO (best-validation 22.7~s) substantially closes this gap, indicating that the architectural changes in FGSv3 — particularly the demand-branch communication signal and factored critic — improve both sample efficiency and policy generalization.

\subsection{SAC convergence and network complexity}

The convergence analysis confirms the theoretical prediction that SAC's sample efficiency advantage over PPO is scenario-dependent. On Cologne Regional ($N=8$), first-generation SAC achieves near-optimal performance in fewer than 40 training episodes, with the policy stabilizing near 6~s by episode~22. On Ingolstadt Regional ($N=21$), the centralized critic's computational overhead terminates training before meaningful learning can occur. This divide aligns with the theoretical analysis in Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv2}: the critic's input dimensionality scales as $O(Nd + NA_\text{max})$ in the number of agents $N$, while PPO's value function input is $O(d)$ independent of $N$. The effective threshold lies between $N=8$ (SAC succeeds) and $N=21$ (SAC fails within resource constraints), confirming that SAC-based MARL with a centralized graph-aware critic is practical for small-to-moderate urban clusters but requires architectural revision to scale further.

The FGSv3 architecture (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv3}) directly addresses this bottleneck. By replacing the centralized critic with a factored neighborhood critic ($O(d_c + A_{\max})$ independent of $N$), FGSv3 reduces the critic input from 3{,}091 to 204 dimensions at ingolstadt21 — a $15\times$ reduction (Appendix~\ref{sec:Appendix_fgsv3_critic_complexity}). Both FGSv2-SAC and FGSv3-SAC complete training at $N=21$. On Cologne Regional ($N=8$), FGSv3-PPO achieves a best-checkpoint mean waiting time of 4.8~s (delay: 21.6~s), marginally better than FGS\textsubscript{S} (wait: 4.9~s, delay: 22.2~s); FGSv3-SAC and FGSv2-SAC are both approximately 6.0~s on this scenario. The FGSv3 architectural benefit is most pronounced on ingolstadt21, where FGSv3-PPO achieves 22.7~s (versus $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$'s 88.6~s, a 74\% improvement), and SAC variants now complete training. The FGSv3-SAC and FGSv2-SAC best-checkpoints on ingolstadt21 (11.1~s and 13.9~s respectively) occur during early training exploration and should be interpreted as indicative lower bounds — stable performance in the converged region of training is approximately 25--28~s for both variants, comparable to standalone PPO (25.8~s). Extended training and hyperparameter tuning are expected to close this gap.

\section{Answering the Research Questions}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_ResearchQuestions}

\noindent\textbf{RQ1: How does the proposed graph-aware multi-agent SAC framework perform compared with classical traffic-control methods and deep RL baselines across German RESCO traffic scenarios?}

On Cologne Regional ($N=8$), $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) achieves 4.9~s, trailing the best single-agent baseline (PPO at 4.3~s) by 14\% while outperforming DQN (6.4~s) by 23\% and the graph-aware baselines CoLight (11.0~s) and standalone FRAP (13.9~s) by 55\% and 64\% respectively. FGSv3-PPO further achieves 4.8~s on this scenario, comparable to standalone PPO. On the Cologne Corridor ($N=3$), FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC) achieves 7.4~s, above the best baselines PPO (6.0~s) and IndSAC (5.5~s) and closely matching CoLight (7.9~s). On Ingolstadt Regional ($N=21$), the first-generation SAC variant terminates prematurely; the PPO variant achieves 88.6~s, better than CoLight (95.2~s) but worse than independent DQN (33.8~s) and PPO (25.8~s). The third-generation FGSv3-PPO achieves 22.7~s on ingolstadt21, outperforming all baselines on this network. Taken together, the FGS framework is competitive on small-to-moderate German scenarios and outperforms graph-aware baselines; FGSv3-PPO is the best-performing variant on the largest tested network.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RQ2: What is the effect of local encoder design and graph-attention communication on traffic signal control performance and training stability?}

Encoder type is the dominant determinant of absolute performance: FRAP-based encoders consistently produce waiting times 5--62$\times$ higher than MLP encoders across all scenarios, due to structural mismatch between FRAP's phase-pair competition assumptions and German intersection heterogeneity. Graph communication type determines the generalization gap: GATv2 reduces the train-to-validation gap on ingolstadt21 substantially compared to GAT, suggesting that dynamic per-edge attention improves policy robustness to demand variation. The interaction between the two components is asymmetric — GATv2 partially compensates for FRAP's encoding failures on cologne8, but this compensation does not extend to larger networks.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RQ3: How does the proposed framework behave across traffic networks of different sizes and topologies?}

$\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) shows consistent performance on Single-intersection scenarios (Cologne: 5.4~s, Ingolstadt: 5.5~s), outperforming standalone FRAP (7.3~s on Ingolstadt Single) and DQN on Cologne Single (6.9~s versus FGS 5.4~s), though DQN converges to comparable performance on Ingolstadt Single (4.8~s versus FGS 5.5~s). On Cologne Regional ($N=8$), $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ achieves the best result among all graph-based methods, and FGSv3-PPO (4.8~s) is the best overall. On Ingolstadt Regional ($N=21$), the first-generation PPO variant achieves 88.6~s — better than CoLight (95.2~s) but substantially worse than standalone PPO (25.8~s) and DQN (33.8~s). FGSv3-PPO achieves 22.7~s on ingolstadt21, outperforming all baselines. Across all scenarios, FGS variants exhibit a consistent pattern: performance improves monotonically as the MLP encoder replaces FRAP, as GATv2 replaces GAT, and as the factored critic replaces the centralized critic.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RQ4 (exploratory): To what extent can the learned multi-agent control behavior be interpreted as decentralized self-organization that produces network-level coordination?}

The GATv2 attention weights assigned to neighborhood intersections represent the agent's implicit model of which neighbors are most informative for its current decision. The convergence trajectories on ingolstadt21 provide indirect evidence of emergent coordination: isolated waiting-time spikes followed by rapid multi-episode recovery indicate that agents propagate demand-spike signals across the attention graph faster than they would recover individually. The FGS(MLP+GATv2+PPO) training trace shows recovery from a 300~s spike within two episodes — a recovery speed that is theoretically possible only if upstream agents adjust their phase plans in response to the demand signal relayed via the GATv2 aggregation step. Full attention-weight visualization and causal attribution are left to future work (Section~\ref{sec:ConclusionFutureWork_FutureResearch}), as the experimental data in this thesis does not include logged attention weights from the GATv2 layer.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Limitations}

Several limitations of the experimental design affect the interpretation of the above results.

\paragraph{Incomplete scenario coverage.}
The baseline runs available in the experiment log cover only a subset of the six RESCO scenarios (Ingolstadt Corridor and all Cologne scenarios are covered for some baselines, but not all). In particular, independent DQN and PPO baselines for the Ingolstadt Corridor ($N=7$) were not compared directly against FGS variants in the runs available at the time of writing, preventing a fully crossed comparison table.

\paragraph{Single seed per run.}
The results in Table~\ref{tab:main_results} correspond to single training runs rather than averages over multiple random seeds. RESCO benchmark protocol specifies five seeds with result averaging; the resource constraints of the available training hardware limited most runs to a single seed. Performance variances across seeds are therefore not reported, and the results should be interpreted as indicative rather than statistically conclusive.

\paragraph{Training instability of SAC variants on ingolstadt21.}
The first-generation $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) on ingolstadt21 terminated after 17 of 200 training episodes due to resource exhaustion. FGSv2-SAC and FGSv3-SAC both complete the full training budget at $N=21$ thanks to the factored critic ($15\times$ reduction in critic input dimensions). However, both SAC variants exhibit high-variance trajectories on ingolstadt21 without clear convergence within 100 episodes: FGSv3-SAC shows a best-checkpoint of 11.1~s at validation episode~3 but then fluctuates between 17~s and 74~s for the remainder of training (stable median $\approx$26~s after episode~6); FGSv2-SAC shows analogous behavior (best 13.9~s at episode~1; stable median $\approx$28~s). These best-checkpoint values are early-training transients driven by SAC's entropy-bonus exploration, not evidence of reliable policy quality. Stable, converged SAC performance on ingolstadt21 would likely require 300--500 training episodes and hyperparameter tuning specific to the 21-agent setting. The FGSv3-PPO variant, by contrast, converges stably (stable median $\approx$26~s from episode~7; best checkpoint 22.7~s), demonstrating that the FGSv3 architecture reliably scales to $N=21$ under the PPO update rule within the available training budget.

\paragraph{Queue-wait discrepancy in SAC early-training checkpoints.}
The best-checkpoint values for FGSv3-SAC and FGSv2-SAC on ingolstadt21 (Table~\ref{tab:fgs_evolution}) exhibit anomalously high queue lengths (12.4 and 12.9 veh./lane) relative to their waiting times (11.1 and 13.9~s). This pattern is consistent with an early-training episode where SAC's aggressive exploration generates a phase schedule that reduces waiting time by rapid cycling but builds long queues. Such early-training anomalies do not reflect the stable operational regime of the trained policy.

\paragraph{Training-to-validation demand shift.}
The train-to-validation performance gap observed for first-generation FGS variants on Ingolstadt Regional (best training wait $\approx$~29~s, best validation wait 88.6~s) is substantially larger than for DQN (best training $\approx$~38~s, best validation 33.8~s). This gap suggests that FGS\textsubscript{P} policies, with their more complex attention-conditioned representations, are more susceptible to demand distribution shift between training and validation seeds. FGSv3-PPO reduces this gap (best validation 22.7~s versus $\approx$25~s training stable range), indicating that the architectural improvements in FGSv3 also improve generalization to unseen demand patterns. Averaging over multiple seeds and using domain-randomization during training remain natural remedies to fully close the generalization gap.
