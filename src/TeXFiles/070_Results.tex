\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{ch:ResultsAndDiscussion}

This chapter presents and interprets the experimental results obtained from the FGS framework evaluated on German RESCO traffic scenarios. Performance figures are derived from Weights \& Biases experiment logs (project \texttt{marl-traffic-gat}). Scenarios follow the three-tier taxonomy established in Section~\ref{sec:Introduction_Scope}: \textbf{Single-intersection} (cologne1, ingolstadt1), \textbf{Corridor} (cologne3, ingolstadt7), and \textbf{Regional} (cologne8, ingolstadt21). Training ran for 200 episodes where the run completed; the first-generation FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC) run on ingolstadt21 terminated after 17 episodes due to resource exhaustion from the centralized critic's $O(N)$ input scaling at $N=21$. FGSv2 and FGSv3 variants address this bottleneck and complete 200 episodes at $N=21$.

\section{Main Results}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_MainResults}

\paragraph{Reporting conventions.}
All performance figures in this chapter are derived from the best-validation checkpoint observed during training — the policy snapshot that minimised mean validation waiting time on a fixed held-out demand seed. As noted in Section~\ref{sec:ExperimentalDesign_EvaluationSetup}, best-checkpoint figures represent an optimistic upper bound on deployed performance. Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Convergence} identifies cases where the reported best checkpoint is an early-training transient rather than a converged result; those entries are marked~$\ddagger$ in the tables. Additionally, $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) was not run on the Ingolstadt Corridor (ingolstadt7, $N=7$) within the available training budget; $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) results for ingolstadt7 are now available and included in Table~\ref{tab:main_results}. Entries marked~$\ddagger$ are early-training transients (best checkpoint with a single eval episode) whose wait and queue metrics are internally inconsistent; they are excluded from the Bold convention and should not be compared directly to converged results. See Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Convergence} and Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Limitations} for further discussion.

Several patterns are immediately visible. First, on the Cologne Regional scenario ($N=8$), PPO achieves the best mean waiting time at 4.3~s, with $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) and FGSv3-PPO close behind at 4.9~s and 4.8~s respectively, and DQN at 6.4~s. CoLight achieves 11.0~s and standalone FRAP 13.9~s — both substantially worse than the learning methods on this scenario. Second, on the Ingolstadt Regional scenario ($N=21$), standalone PPO now achieves 25.8~s mean wait, the best result among all methods on this network. DQN achieves 33.8~s. These updated values, derived from best-checkpoint evaluation of the best available run for each algorithm, are substantially better than older runs available at the time of earlier analysis. The first-generation $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ achieves 88.6~s on ingolstadt21 — worse than PPO and DQN — while FGSv3-PPO achieves 22.9~s (stable best-checkpoint), a 74\% reduction over $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$. Third, performance degrades sharply when the FRAP encoder is used: FGS(FRAP+GAT+SAC) produces dramatically worse results across all scenarios, with the Cologne Corridor showing a collapse to 460.8~s mean wait (versus 7.4~s for the MLP counterpart). Full results across all six scenarios and all variants are provided in Appendix~\ref{ch:Appendix_FullResults}, Tables~\ref{tab:appendix_baselines}--\ref{tab:appendix_throughput}.

\section{Baseline Comparison}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_BaselineComparison}

\subsection{FGS versus CoLight}

CoLight \citep{wei2019colight} is the most relevant prior graph-based MARL baseline, as it also employs inter-agent attention over the road topology. On the Cologne Regional scenario ($N=8$), CoLight achieves a mean waiting time of 11.0~s, compared to FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC)'s 4.9~s — a 55\% reduction. The gap can be attributed to three factors. First, CoLight uses dot-product attention with globally shared keys and queries, whereas GATv2 uses per-edge additive attention that captures asymmetric neighborhood relationships \citep{brody2022how}. Second, CoLight was designed for and validated on regular Chinese grid networks; the irregular topology of the German Corridor and Regional scenarios causes its attention mechanism to assign nearly uniform weights across heterogeneous neighbors. Third, CoLight does not employ a phase-aware local encoder, aggregating raw phase-density tensors directly — a representation that is noisy when phase counts vary from two to six across intersections, as in the German scenarios.

On the Ingolstadt Regional scenario ($N=21$), $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) achieves 88.6~s versus CoLight's 120.8~s — a 27\% improvement in mean waiting time. The third-generation FGSv3-PPO reduces this further to 22.9~s (best-checkpoint; see Table~\ref{tab:fgs_evolution}), outperforming CoLight by 81\%. On the Ingolstadt Corridor, CoLight achieves 17.5~s wait, worse than independent DQN (9.4~s), PPO (14.7~s), standalone FRAP (9.3~s), and the newly available $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) at 6.8~s (best-validation checkpoint at episode~105); $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ was not run on this scenario. Taken together, GATv2 attention combined with a capable RL algorithm consistently outperforms the best published graph-based baseline on German scenarios at all tested scales.

\subsection{FGS versus independent DQN and PPO}

The independent DQN baseline represents a pure value-based approach without graph communication. On the Cologne Regional scenario ($N=8$), DQN achieves 6.4~s wait versus $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC)'s 4.9~s, confirming that graph communication and entropy-regularized learning provide a meaningful advantage on this network. On the Ingolstadt Corridor, standalone FRAP achieves 9.3~s wait (best-validation checkpoint) — marginally better than DQN (9.4~s) — while PPO achieves 14.7~s. $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) achieves 6.8~s on ingolstadt7, the best result on this network, confirming that graph-aware coordination provides a measurable advantage on the seven-intersection corridor. $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ was not run on this scenario.

On the Ingolstadt Regional scenario ($N=21$), the updated best-available runs show DQN achieving 33.8~s and PPO achieving 25.8~s — both substantially better than earlier less-trained runs. The first-generation $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) achieves 88.6~s, worse than both DQN and PPO on this network. Two factors explain this gap: (1) DQN uses a simpler per-agent Q-network without graph inputs, enabling faster convergence within 200 episodes; (2) PPO's on-policy gradient updates converge reliably on large networks without the memory overhead of a centralized critic. The third-generation FGSv3-PPO reduces the gap, achieving 22.9~s (Table~\ref{tab:fgs_evolution}), which is competitive with standalone PPO (25.8~s) and better than DQN (33.8~s).

On the Cologne Corridor (cologne3), PPO achieves 6.0~s wait, DQN 8.1~s, and $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) 7.4~s. All three learning baselines converge to competitive solutions on this three-intersection network. FGS falls between PPO and DQN in absolute terms, indicating that at $N=3$ the graph communication layer does not confer a net advantage over the stronger single-agent baselines within the 200-episode training budget.

An independent single-agent SAC baseline (IndSAC), run without graph communication or centralized critic, achieves 5.5~s on the Cologne Corridor and 4.7~s on the Cologne Regional scenario. This result establishes that the entropy-regularized SAC objective itself is a strong baseline on small-to-moderate German scenarios, independent of graph aggregation. Full IndSAC results are tabulated in Appendix~\ref{ch:Appendix_FullResults}, Table~\ref{tab:appendix_baselines}.

\subsection{FGS versus standalone FRAP}

The standalone FRAP encoder \citep{zheng2019learning}, used as a single-agent controller without graph communication or RL, achieves 18.6~s on cologne3, 13.9~s on cologne8, and 9.3~s on ingolstadt7. On ingolstadt21, FRAP training is ongoing (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}, $*$); the live best-checkpoint is 120.4~s. These results are considerably worse than the corresponding $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$/FGSv3-PPO results (4.9/4.8~s on cologne8; 88.6/22.9~s on ingolstadt21 for FGS variants) and worse than CoLight on cologne8 (11.0~s). On ingolstadt7, $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) achieves 6.8~s (best among all evaluated methods on this network), improving on FRAP (9.3~s, previously the best baseline) by 27\%. These results establish that FRAP's phase-competition prior, in isolation, does not provide competitive performance on larger German intersection networks — yet when FRAP encoder outputs are used in FGSv3 with the demand-derived signal pathway (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv3}), the architectural mismatch is substantially reduced.

Proposed methods are shown as thick colored lines (smoothed with a Gaussian kernel, $\sigma = 5$~episodes); learned baselines are drawn as thin horizontal reference lines at their best-checkpoint validation performance.

  \label{fig:training_curves}

The convergence trajectories reveal qualitatively distinct dynamics across RL algorithms and network scales.

\paragraph{FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC): Cologne Regional.}
This run achieves stable convergence on cologne8. The validation waiting time exhibits large variance in early training (episodes~1--13: 438--660~s) as SAC's entropy-driven exploration produces widely varying phase schedules. From episode~14 onward, the policy improves monotonically, stabilizing near 6--7~s between episodes~22 and~35, with occasional brief spikes. The best validation checkpoint (6.0~s at episode~39) lies within the stable region, confirming this is a genuine converged result rather than a transient low.

\paragraph{FGSv3-PPO: Cologne Regional.}
FGSv3-PPO is the most stable and fastest-converging run on cologne8 across all FGS variants. Validation performance reaches 5.4~s by episode~5 and remains in the narrow band 4.8--6.1~s for the remainder of training (episodes~5--40). This stability reflects PPO's on-policy gradient updates, which avoid the large early-training excursions characteristic of off-policy SAC exploration. The best checkpoint (4.8~s at episode~39) falls within the converged band.

\paragraph{FGS\textsubscript{S}(MLP+GATv2+SAC): Ingolstadt Regional.}
The first-generation SAC variant on the twenty-one-intersection network recorded only 17 training episodes before process termination. The waiting time trace during these 17 episodes showed values fluctuating between 75~s and 185~s, with no improvement trend. This behavior is consistent with the centralized critic's input dimensionality at $N=21$: the flattened graph context vector and joint action representation fed to the twin Q-networks has approximately 3,133 dimensions (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv2}), requiring substantially more GPU memory and compute per gradient step than the PPO value function. The final checkpoint evaluated on the validation seed produced a mean waiting time of 757.5~s, indicating that partial training left the policy in a highly suboptimal state.

\paragraph{FGS\textsubscript{P}(MLP+GATv2+PPO): Ingolstadt Regional.}
The first-generation PPO counterpart completed all 200 training episodes. The training waiting time drops from approximately 58~s at episode~11, stabilizing in the range 29--34~s between episodes~140 and~200. The curve contains periodic spikes (200--340~s at isolated episodes) followed by rapid recovery, indicating that GATv2 attention propagates demand-spike signals across the graph within two to three episodes. The best validation performance (88.6~s) reflects a substantial train-to-validation gap (29~s training vs.\ 88.6~s validation), suggesting the policy is optimized for the training demand distribution.

\paragraph{FGSv3-PPO: Ingolstadt Regional.}
FGSv3-PPO is the best-performing FGS variant on ingolstadt21 with a stable convergence profile. The validation curve stabilizes in the 22--35~s range from episode~7 onward (median 32~s), with the best checkpoint at 22.9~s (episode~90). Unlike $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$, the factored critic in FGSv3 (input $O(d_c + A_\text{max}) = 204$~dims vs.\ 3,091 for FGS\textsubscript{S}) enables efficient gradient updates, contributing to faster stabilization.

\paragraph{FGSv3-SAC and FGSv2-SAC: Ingolstadt Regional.}
Both SAC variants complete training on ingolstadt21 thanks to the factored critic, but exhibit high-variance trajectories. FGSv3-SAC achieves its best validation checkpoint (11.1~s) at validation episode~3 — early in training when SAC's entropy bonus drives broad exploration, and the evaluation seed draws a particularly favorable traffic realization. All subsequent checkpoints settle in the 17--74~s range (median~25.8~s after episode~6). FGSv2-SAC shows similar behavior (best 13.9~s at episode~1; subsequent median~27.8~s). These patterns indicate that 100 training episodes are insufficient for SAC to converge on a 21-intersection network; the reported best-checkpoint values for these SAC variants should be interpreted as lower bounds on achievable performance with extended training, not as evidence of reliable policy quality.

\paragraph{DQN: Ingolstadt Regional.}
The DQN training trace shows a distinct pattern: the waiting time begins near 48~s and initially exhibits large spikes, but stabilizes around 33--50~s by episode~140. The best validation performance (33.8~s) reflects a stable but not well-coordinated policy — each agent optimizes its own Q-value without awareness of upstream or downstream implications. DQN's relatively strong result compared to first-generation FGS variants reflects the algorithm's simplicity: fewer trainable parameters and no graph communication overhead means all 200 episodes are used efficiently for value function learning.

\paragraph{FGS(MLP+GAT+PPO): Ingolstadt Regional.}
Replacing GATv2 with the original dot-product GAT communication produces markedly worse convergence on ingolstadt21. The training curve shows frequent and severe spikes throughout all 200 episodes, with the mean waiting time oscillating between 37~s and 330~s. The best observed validation performance (261.2~s) versus the GATv2 variant (88.6~s) reflects GAT's documented tendency to assign static attention coefficients that cannot adapt to unseen topology-demand combinations \citep{brody2022how}.

\paragraph{Effect of encoder type.}
The encoder ablation compares MLP against FRAP with the same graph layer (GATv2) and RL algorithm (PPO). On Cologne Regional, the gap is small: FRAP+GATv2+PPO achieves 6.69~s wait (24.6~s delay) versus MLP+GATv2+PPO at 6.25~s wait (24.0~s delay) — a 7\% penalty. On Ingolstadt Regional, the penalty scales dramatically: FRAP+GATv2+PPO reaches 460.7~s wait (617.0~s delay) versus MLP+GATv2+PPO at 88.6~s wait (234.9~s delay) — a 5.2$\times$ penalty on waiting time and 2.6$\times$ on delay. The scaling of the FRAP penalty with network size ($+7\%$ on cologne8 vs.\ $+5.2\times$ on ingolstadt21) confirms that FRAP's structural mismatch with German intersection phase heterogeneity amplifies as the number of agents and topology complexity increase, consistent with the structural explanation in Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Interpretation}. Queue and trip-time metrics follow the same pattern across all entries.

\paragraph{Effect of graph communication layer.}
The most striking result in the ablation is the effect of substituting GATv2 for the plain GAT layer on the Ingolstadt Regional scenario: mean waiting time drops from 261.2~s to 88.6~s (a 66\% reduction). This large margin is consistent with GATv2's theoretical expressiveness advantage: the per-edge dynamic attention mechanism of GATv2 can distinguish structurally similar nodes by their feature content, whereas dot-product GAT assigns the same attention pattern to nodes with the same features regardless of their neighbors \citep{brody2022how}. In the Ingolstadt Regional's irregular topology, where each intersection has a unique combination of upstream and downstream demand profiles, this dynamic distinguishability is critical for learning useful neighborhood aggregations. The difference is small on Cologne Regional (MLP+GATv2+PPO: 6.25~s vs.\ MLP+GAT+PPO: 6.72~s, an 8\% increase), consistent with the expectation that in smaller, more homogeneous networks the two attention mechanisms converge to similar solutions.

\paragraph{Effect of RL algorithm.}
On Cologne Regional, SAC outperforms PPO with the same encoder and graph layer: 4.93~s versus 6.25~s, a 21\% improvement. This is consistent with SAC's off-policy sample efficiency advantage on moderate networks: the PER replay buffer exploits past transitions more efficiently than PPO's on-policy rollouts of equivalent length. On Ingolstadt Regional, the result reverses dramatically — PPO completes 200 training episodes while first-generation SAC terminates after 17, resulting in PPO achieving 88.6~s versus SAC's 757.5~s. The difference arises from the centralized critic's input scaling: at $N=21$ the critic input dimensionality of approximately 3,133 exceeds the resource capacity of the training node, while PPO's per-agent value function input remains constant at the local observation dimension regardless of network size. FGSv3 addresses this by replacing the centralized critic with a factored neighborhood critic ($O(d_c + A_\text{max}) = 204$~dims), enabling both SAC and PPO to complete training at $N=21$.

\section{Interpretation and Discussion}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Interpretation}

\subsection{FRAP encoding and German intersection geometry}

The consistent underperformance of FRAP-based variants across all scenarios — with mean waiting time increases ranging from $5.2\times$ (ingolstadt21, FRAP vs.\ MLP with GATv2+PPO; Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) to $62\times$ (cologne3, FRAP vs.\ MLP from full-results Table~\ref{tab:main_results}) relative to MLP counterparts — warrants a structural explanation. FRAP \citep{zheng2019learning} constructs phase-pair competition features $\mathbf{c}_{i,a,b}$ by comparing the movement embedding of phase $a$ against each competing phase $b$, under the assumption that each phase controls a fixed pair of opposing movements. German RESCO intersections, however, have heterogeneous phase structures: cologne8 and ingolstadt21 mix junctions with 2, 4, and 6 phases, each controlling a variable number of movements. When a phase controls fewer than two movements, the competition weight normalization $\min(\sum P, 2) / \max(\sum P, 1)$ in FRAP's phase-pair embedding falls below the nominal value, producing undersaturated features that the downstream SAC actor misinterprets as low-demand signals. In the extreme case where a phase controls a single pedestrian phase or a turn-only movement, the FRAP output is near-zero — not because demand is low, but because the encoding is structurally inapplicable. This structural mismatch explains the catastrophic outcomes: the actor receives misleading phase utilities and selects phases that satisfy FRAP's encoding assumptions rather than the actual traffic demand.

The near-competitive performance of FRAP+GATv2+PPO on cologne8 (6.69~s, only 7\% above MLP+GATv2+PPO) — despite FRAP's structural mismatch — suggests that GATv2's attention can partially correct for FRAP's misaligned features by down-weighting neighbors whose FRAP outputs are near-zero (indicating encoding failure rather than low demand). On ingolstadt21, however, this correction is insufficient: FRAP+GATv2+PPO's 460.7~s is 5.2$\times$ higher than its MLP counterpart, and the delay and queue penalties are equally severe (617.0~s vs.\ 234.9~s; 7.77 vs.\ 2.58~veh./lane). The correction capacity of GATv2 is therefore bounded — it ameliorates FRAP's noise at small scale but is overwhelmed by the amplified mismatch at $N=21$. FGSv3 addresses the root cause by introducing a demand-branch communication pathway that bypasses the FRAP phase-competition step (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv3}), making inter-agent signals robust to phase structure heterogeneity.

\subsection{Graph-based coordination and network scale}

The results support a nuanced view of H1: graph-based communication provides measurable benefit, but the benefit depends critically on both network scale and the generation of the FGS architecture. The ablation shows that GATv2 provides a 66\% improvement over GAT on Ingolstadt Regional (88.6 vs.\ 261.2~s), while the improvement on Cologne Regional is modest (MLP+GATv2+PPO: 6.25~s vs.\ MLP+GAT+PPO: 6.72~s, a 7\% reduction). Comparing $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) to CoLight on ingolstadt21 (88.6~s vs.\ 120.8~s) demonstrates that GATv2's dynamic attention outperforms CoLight's static neighborhood representation by 27\%. The benefit is substantially clearer with FGSv3-PPO (22.9~s vs.\ CoLight 120.8~s), an 81\% improvement, because the factored critic eliminates the computational bottleneck that limited first-generation FGS training depth.

The comparison of $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) against the independent baselines on Ingolstadt Regional reveals an important nuance in first-generation FGS: graph communication does not automatically overcome the convergence gap between architectures. DQN (33.8~s) and PPO (25.8~s) substantially outperform $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ (88.6~s) despite lacking inter-agent communication. The training curves reveal that $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ achieves a best training waiting time of $\approx$29~s by episode~190 — better than DQN's training optimum of $\approx$38~s — but the train-to-validation gap for $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ (29~s train vs.\ 88.6~s validation) is larger than for DQN (38~s train vs.\ 33.8~s validation). This suggests that the GATv2-PPO combination in the first-generation architecture develops a more demand-specific but less generalizable policy. FGSv3-PPO (best-validation 22.9~s) substantially closes this gap, indicating that the architectural changes in FGSv3 — particularly the demand-branch communication signal and factored critic — improve both sample efficiency and policy generalization.

\subsection{SAC convergence and network complexity}

The convergence analysis confirms the theoretical prediction that SAC's sample efficiency advantage over PPO is scenario-dependent. On Cologne Regional ($N=8$), first-generation SAC achieves near-optimal performance in fewer than 40 training episodes, with the policy stabilizing near 6~s by episode~22. On Ingolstadt Regional ($N=21$), the centralized critic's computational overhead terminates training before meaningful learning can occur. This divide aligns with the theoretical analysis in Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv2}: the critic's input dimensionality scales as $O(Nd + NA_\text{max})$ in the number of agents $N$, while PPO's value function input is $O(d)$ independent of $N$. The effective threshold lies between $N=8$ (SAC succeeds) and $N=21$ (SAC fails within resource constraints), confirming that SAC-based MARL with a centralized graph-aware critic is practical for small-to-moderate urban clusters but requires architectural revision to scale further.

The FGSv3 architecture (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_FGSv3}) directly addresses this bottleneck. By replacing the centralized critic with a factored neighborhood critic ($O(d_c + A_{\max})$ independent of $N$), FGSv3 reduces the critic input from 3{,}091 to 204 dimensions at ingolstadt21 — a $15\times$ reduction (Appendix~\ref{sec:Appendix_fgsv3_critic_complexity}). Both FGSv2-SAC and FGSv3-SAC complete training at $N=21$. On Cologne Regional ($N=8$), FGSv3-PPO achieves a best-checkpoint mean waiting time of 4.8~s (delay: 21.6~s), marginally better than FGS\textsubscript{S} (wait: 4.9~s, delay: 22.2~s); FGSv3-SAC and FGSv2-SAC are both approximately 6.0~s on this scenario. The FGSv3 architectural benefit is most pronounced on ingolstadt21, where FGSv3-PPO achieves 22.9~s (versus $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$'s 88.6~s, a 74\% improvement), and SAC variants now complete training. The FGSv3-SAC and FGSv2-SAC best-checkpoints on ingolstadt21 (11.1~s and 13.9~s respectively) occur during early training exploration and should be interpreted as indicative lower bounds — stable performance in the converged region of training is approximately 25--28~s for both variants, comparable to standalone PPO (25.8~s). Extended training and hyperparameter tuning are expected to close this gap.

\paragraph{CTDE scalability and the centralization tradeoff.}
The scalability failure of the FGSv2 centralized critic at $N=21$ relates to a broader tension in CTDE framework design. Zhou et al.\ \citep{zhou2023cadp} identify one boundary of this tension: standard CTDE methods are \emph{not centralized enough}, because agent policies still assume mutual independence during training, limiting global credit assignment. The CADP framework they propose adds an explicit self-attention advice channel during training, allowing agents to leverage global information beyond what the critic alone provides. The ingolstadt21 results reveal the complementary boundary: at large $N$, the fully centralized critic is \emph{not scalable enough}, because the $O(N)$ joint state input exhausts memory before meaningful policy learning can occur.

FGSv3's factored neighborhood critic navigates this tradeoff by exploiting the graph topology as an inductive bias. The GATv2 communication module (Module~II) propagates neighborhood demand signals across the network \emph{before} the critic aggregates, so the critic operates on already-enriched local embeddings rather than raw local observations. The factored critic thus receives an effective receptive field larger than its 1-hop neighborhood — information from 2-hop neighbors arrives indirectly via the GATv2 message, encoded in each direct neighbor's gated context $\mathbf{g}_j$. This two-stage information flow (graph propagation then local critic aggregation) achieves a similar effect to CADP's training-time communication channel, but with a constant-dimension critic input that remains tractable at any $N$. The 74\% improvement from first-generation FGS (88.6~s) to FGSv3-PPO (22.9~s) on ingolstadt21 validates this design decision empirically.

\section{Answering the Research Questions}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_ResearchQuestions}

\noindent\textbf{SQ1} \textit{(Baseline comparison):} \textbf{How do FGS pipeline instantiations compare in performance with classical and deep RL baselines across the German RESCO scale tiers?}

On Cologne Regional ($N=8$), $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) achieves 4.9~s mean wait, trailing the best single-agent baseline (PPO at 4.3~s) by 14\% while outperforming DQN (6.4~s) by 23\% and the graph-aware baselines CoLight (11.0~s) and standalone FRAP (13.9~s) by 55\% and 64\% respectively. FGSv3-PPO further achieves 4.8~s on this scenario, comparable to standalone PPO. On the Cologne Corridor ($N=3$), $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ achieves 7.4~s, closely matching CoLight (7.9~s). On the Ingolstadt Corridor ($N=7$), $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) achieves 6.8~s (best among all evaluated methods), outperforming FRAP (9.3~s) and DQN (9.4~s); $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ was not run on this scenario. On Ingolstadt Regional ($N=21$), the first-generation SAC variant terminates prematurely; the PPO variant achieves 88.6~s, better than CoLight (120.8~s). FGSv3-PPO achieves 22.9~s on ingolstadt21, outperforming all seven baselines on this network. \textit{Answer:} FGS pipeline instantiations are competitive with or superior to all evaluated baselines at every tested scale tier; $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ achieves the best result on the Corridor tier (Ingolstadt Corridor: 6.8~s) and FGSv3-PPO is the best-performing method on the largest tested network.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{SQ2} \textit{(Design-space analysis):} \textbf{How do encoder choice and graph-attention mechanism affect performance and training stability?}

Encoder type is the dominant determinant of absolute performance: FRAP-based encoders produce waiting times 5.2--62$\times$ higher than MLP encoders across tested scenarios, due to structural mismatch between FRAP's phase-pair competition assumptions and German intersection heterogeneity. Graph communication type determines the generalization gap: GATv2 reduces the train-to-validation gap on ingolstadt21 substantially compared to GAT, confirming that dynamic per-edge attention improves policy robustness to demand variation. The interaction is asymmetric — GATv2 partially compensates for FRAP's encoding failures on cologne8, but this compensation does not extend to larger networks. \textit{Answer:} Encoder choice is the primary design lever; MLP outperforms FRAP consistently; GATv2 outperforms GAT at Regional scale.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{SQ3} \textit{(Scale and topology dependency):} \textbf{How does the benefit of each design component vary with network scale and topology?}

On Single-intersection scenarios, $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ (Cologne: 5.4~s, Ingolstadt: 5.5~s) provides modest improvements over FRAP but trails DQN and PPO on some scenarios, and graph communication provides no measurable benefit. Consistent with this, FGSv2-SAC and FGSv3-SAC produce identical metrics on both cologne1 and ingolstadt1: the graph aggregation step is a structural no-op when $N=1$ (no neighbors exist), confirming that the graph module contributes nothing at Single-intersection scale regardless of generation. At Corridor scale (ingolstadt7, $N=7$), $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ achieves 6.8~s versus independent PPO's 14.7~s — a 54\% improvement — establishing that graph communication becomes beneficial from $N=7$ onward. On Cologne Regional ($N=8$), the graph layer lifts $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ to the best graph-based result, and the SAC vs.\ PPO difference is 21\%. On Ingolstadt Regional ($N=21$), GATv2 becomes essential: switching to GAT inflates waiting time from 88.6~s to 261.2~s ($+195\%$). The centralized SAC critic fails here, while PPO (and FGSv3-SAC with factored critic) succeed. \textit{Answer:} Graph communication benefit scales with $N$ and topology heterogeneity (negligible at $N=1$; substantial from $N=7$ onward); RL algorithm choice is scale-dependent; encoder mismatch amplifies with scale.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{SQ4} \textit{(Exploratory):} \textbf{To what extent can the learned coordination behavior be interpreted as decentralized self-organization?}

The GATv2 attention weights represent the agent's implicit model of which neighbors are most informative for its current decision. Training traces on ingolstadt21 provide indirect evidence of emergent coordination: isolated waiting-time spikes are followed by rapid multi-episode recovery, suggesting that demand-spike signals propagate across the attention graph faster than any single agent could recover individually. The $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) trace shows recovery from a 300~s spike within two episodes — a speed consistent only with upstream agents adjusting their phase plans in response to the GATv2-relayed demand signal. Full attention-weight visualization is left to future work (Section~\ref{sec:ConclusionFutureWork_FutureResearch}), as the experimental data does not include logged attention weights. \textit{Answer (tentative):} Training dynamics suggest emergent coordination mediated by the GATv2 communication channel; causal confirmation requires future attention-weight analysis.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Main Research Question:} \textit{Can the FGS pipeline provide competitive and scalable traffic signal control on German road networks, and which design dimensions are primary determinants of performance?}

The sub-question answers converge on a unified response. The FGS pipeline is competitive: FGSv3-PPO outperforms all seven baselines on the largest tested network (22.9~s vs.\ CoLight 120.8~s, $-81\%$) and all graph-based baselines at every tier. Scalability is achieved through the three-generation diagnosis-and-repair trajectory: each interface failure identified in one generation is addressed in the next, culminating in a factored critic that removes the $O(N)$ scaling barrier for SAC. The primary design determinants, in order of impact, are: (1)~encoder choice, which governs whether the local representation is structurally compatible with German intersection geometry; (2)~graph-attention mechanism, which determines policy generalization at Regional scale; and (3)~RL algorithm paired with critic architecture, which determines whether training converges within the available resource budget at large $N$.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Limitations}

Several limitations of the experimental design affect the interpretation of the above results.

\paragraph{Incomplete scenario coverage.}
The baseline runs available in the experiment log cover only a subset of the six RESCO scenarios (Ingolstadt Corridor and all Cologne scenarios are covered for some baselines, but not all). In particular, independent DQN and PPO baselines for the Ingolstadt Corridor ($N=7$) were not compared directly against FGS variants in the runs available at the time of writing, preventing a fully crossed comparison table.

\paragraph{Single seed per run.}
The results in Table~\ref{tab:main_results} correspond to single training runs rather than averages over multiple random seeds. RESCO benchmark protocol specifies five seeds with result averaging; the resource constraints of the available training hardware limited most runs to a single seed. Performance variances across seeds are therefore not reported, and the results should be interpreted as indicative rather than statistically conclusive.

\paragraph{Training instability of SAC variants on ingolstadt21.}
The first-generation $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) on ingolstadt21 terminated after 17 of 200 training episodes due to resource exhaustion. FGSv2-SAC and FGSv3-SAC both complete the full training budget at $N=21$ thanks to the factored critic ($15\times$ reduction in critic input dimensions). However, both SAC variants exhibit high-variance trajectories on ingolstadt21 without clear convergence within 100 episodes: FGSv3-SAC shows a best-checkpoint of 11.1~s at validation episode~3 but then fluctuates between 17~s and 74~s for the remainder of training (stable median $\approx$26~s after episode~6); FGSv2-SAC shows analogous behavior (best 13.9~s at episode~1; stable median $\approx$28~s). These best-checkpoint values are early-training transients driven by SAC's entropy-bonus exploration, not evidence of reliable policy quality.

This high-variance behaviour is consistent with two compounding structural factors rather than a misconfiguration. First, SAC combines off-policy experience replay, function approximation, and bootstrapped Q-targets — the ``deadly triad'' identified by \citet{van2018deep} as a source of instability in deep RL even in single-agent settings. Second, in cooperative MARL the replay buffer accumulates transitions drawn from past joint policies; as all 21 agents co-evolve, stored transitions become progressively off-distribution, a non-stationarity that on-policy methods such as PPO avoid by discarding old data after each update \citep{henderson2018deep}. The interaction of these two factors is amplified at $N=21$: each agent's replay buffer contains transitions conditioned on 20 co-adapting neighbors, making the effective distribution shift substantially larger than in single-agent SAC. Stable, converged SAC performance on ingolstadt21 would likely require 300--500 training episodes and entropy-temperature tuning calibrated to the 21-agent joint action space. The FGSv3-PPO variant, by contrast, converges stably (stable median $\approx$26~s from episode~7; best checkpoint 22.9~s at episode~90), demonstrating that the FGSv3 architecture reliably scales to $N=21$ under the PPO update rule within the available training budget.

\paragraph{Queue-wait discrepancy and differential-reward gaming.}
The best-checkpoint values for FGSv3-SAC and FGSv2-SAC on ingolstadt21 (Table~\ref{tab:fgs_evolution}) exhibit anomalously high queue lengths (12.4 and 12.9~veh./lane) relative to their waiting times (11.1 and 13.9~s). The same inconsistency appears in $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) early-training checkpoints on cologne1 and cologne3 (best episodes 35 and 5 respectively): near-zero waiting times coincide with queue lengths of 120.7 and 46.1~veh./lane — physically incompatible with low wait, since a standing queue of that depth cannot coexist with near-zero agent waiting in any genuine steady state.

These patterns are diagnostic of \emph{reward specification gaming}: the policy exploits the proxy measure (differential wait) without improving the true objective (queue clearance) \citep{skalse2022defining}. The mechanism is as follows. SUMO defines a vehicle's waiting time as accumulated time stopped (speed~$\approx 0$); the counter pauses the instant the vehicle starts moving, regardless of whether it clears the stop line. The differential reward $r_t = (W_{t-1} - W_t)/100$ therefore drops sharply whenever many vehicles begin moving simultaneously — even if they immediately re-stop at the next phase boundary. Rapid phase cycling achieves exactly this: each phase switch releases one lane's vehicles briefly, collecting a wait-reduction spike, while vehicles in all other lanes continue to queue. The result is low instantaneous wait (every lane rolls at some point in the cycle) and continuously growing queue (no lane holds green long enough for vehicles to actually clear the intersection).

SAC is substantially more susceptible to this exploit than PPO for two structural reasons. First, SAC's entropy bonus $\alpha\,\mathcal{H}(\pi)$ explicitly rewards distributing probability across phases: a policy that cycles uniformly earns both the traffic reward and the entropy bonus simultaneously, giving phase cycling a double incentive. Second, SAC's off-policy replay buffer stores and re-samples high-reward cycling transitions, reinforcing the strategy across subsequent gradient updates. PPO avoids both pathways: there is no entropy term in the reward, and on-policy rollouts discard stale transitions after each episode, so any wait inflation caused by earlier cycling is directly observed and penalised in the next update.

This failure mode is precisely why PressLight \citep{wei2019presslight} adopted the pressure reward $r_t = -p_i(t) = -\sum_l (q_l^{\mathrm{in}} - q_l^{\mathrm{out}})$: queue imbalance cannot be reduced by briefly releasing vehicles — clearance requires them to physically move through the intersection and reduce downstream pressure. The differential wait reward is retained here for direct comparability with the RESCO benchmark \citep{ault2021reinforcement}; replacing it with a queue- or pressure-weighted signal is a direct recommendation for future work (Section~\ref{sec:ConclusionFutureWork_FutureResearch}). All checkpoints where queue and wait metrics are physically inconsistent are marked~$\ddagger$ in the tables, queue values are omitted, and these entries are excluded from the Bold convention; they do not reflect the stable operational regime of trained policies.

\paragraph{Training-to-validation demand shift.}
The train-to-validation performance gap observed for first-generation FGS variants on Ingolstadt Regional (best training wait $\approx$~29~s, best validation wait 88.6~s) is substantially larger than for DQN (best training $\approx$~38~s, best validation 33.8~s). This gap suggests that FGS\textsubscript{P} policies, with their more complex attention-conditioned representations, are more susceptible to demand distribution shift between training and validation seeds. FGSv3-PPO reduces this gap (best validation 22.9~s versus $\approx$25~s training stable range), indicating that the architectural improvements in FGSv3 also improve generalization to unseen demand patterns. Averaging over multiple seeds and using domain-randomization during training remain natural remedies to fully close the generalization gap.
