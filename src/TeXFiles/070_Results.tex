\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{ch:ResultsAndDiscussion}

This chapter presents and interprets the experimental results of the FGS framework across six German RESCO scenarios, organised around the central finding that the benefit of graph-aware inter-agent communication grows systematically with network scale and topological complexity. Mean delay (MD, Equation~\ref{eq:delay}) is the primary evaluation metric throughout: it isolates time lost to congestion relative to the free-flow baseline and is less susceptible to the reward-specification artefacts described in Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Limitations}. All reported figures are best-validation checkpoints from single-seed runs (seed~0). The full four-metric results table (mean delay, mean waiting time, mean trip time, throughput) is provided in Appendix~\ref{ch:Appendix_FullResults}.

\section{Scale-Dependent Performance}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_ScaleDependency}
%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The central finding of this thesis is that the benefit of graph-aware inter-agent communication grows systematically with network scale and topological complexity. This section provides the empirical evidence and the theoretical grounding for that claim.

\paragraph{Spatio-temporal dependencies in traffic networks.}
Traffic networks are spatially coupled dynamical systems. A red phase at one intersection blocks vehicles from reaching the next, inducing queue spillback that reduces the effective green time available to upstream controllers~\citep{daganzo1994cell}. As network size increases, these spatial interactions span multiple hops: a demand surge on one arterial can propagate congestion across several intersections before dissipating~\citep{chen2020toward}. Geroliminis and Daganzo~\citep{geroliminis2008existence} demonstrated empirically that network-level flow--density relationships emerge as macroscopic phenomena from local spatial interactions, establishing that urban traffic behaves as a coupled system rather than a collection of independent intersections. Graph neural networks have been applied to traffic forecasting precisely because the graph structure of road networks captures these spatial dependencies~\citep{yu2018spatio}; the FGS framework extends this insight to the signal control problem by using GATv2 message passing to communicate demand signals between neighbouring intersections at each decision step.

At small scale ($N \leq 3$), local observations capture most of the relevant demand context: an individual agent can infer queue propagation from its own lane detectors. At larger scale ($N \geq 7$), the multi-hop dependency structure exceeds the local observation horizon, and explicit inter-agent communication becomes necessary to achieve coordinated flow~\citep{chen2020toward,wei2019colight}. Independent agents optimising local rewards ignore upstream and downstream consequences, producing coordination failures that manifest as network-wide congestion~\citep{ault2021reinforcement}.

\paragraph{Empirical scale benefit.}
Figure~\ref{fig:scale_benefit} quantifies this pattern directly. The x-axis represents network scale tier by agent count; the y-axis shows the percentage improvement in mean delay of the best available FGS variant over (a)~CoLight and (b)~standalone PPO. At $N=1$, the graph module is a structural no-op: no neighbours exist and the GATv2 aggregation returns an identity transformation. The FGS advantage over CoLight grows from near-zero at Single-intersection scale to 68\% at Regional scale on ingolstadt21 (68.4~s vs.\ 216.8~s mean delay). Against standalone PPO, FGSv3-PPO closes the gap from a deficit at small scale to approximate parity at cologne8 ($\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ 22.2~s vs.\ PPO 20.8~s) and a 19\% improvement on ingolstadt21 (68.4~s vs.\ 85.0~s).

\paragraph{Network-level interpretation: Macroscopic Fundamental Diagram.}
Figure~\ref{fig:mfd_ingolstadt21} connects the RL performance results to traffic engineering theory through the Macroscopic Fundamental Diagram (MFD)~\citep{geroliminis2008existence}. The MFD relates network-level vehicle density (vehicles/km) to network outflow (vehicles/h); a controller that keeps the network near the capacity-flow regime (peak of the MFD curve) produces the most efficient throughput. FGSv3-PPO maintains the ingolstadt21 network in the free-flow region for a greater proportion of the evaluation episode than FixedTime, DQN, or PPO, consistent with its graph-aware coordination preventing the queue spillback that pushes the network into hypercongestion~\citep{knoop2012relationship}. This analysis connects the mean delay improvements in Table~\ref{tab:main_results} to a theoretically grounded traffic-engineering explanation: the GATv2 communication layer allows agents to anticipate upstream pressure and preempt spillback conditions that local observation alone cannot detect.

\section{Baseline Comparison}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_BaselineComparison}
%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Three structural comparisons illuminate the mechanisms behind the scale-dependent pattern.

\paragraph{FGS versus CoLight.}
CoLight~\citep{wei2019colight} uses dot-product attention with globally shared keys and queries; GATv2~\citep{brody2022how} uses per-edge additive attention that assigns distinct weights to structurally similar nodes based on their feature content. On the irregular topology of ingolstadt21 --- where each intersection has a unique combination of upstream demand profiles and phase counts --- GATv2's dynamic distinguishability is essential. CoLight's shared-weight attention assigns nearly uniform coefficients across heterogeneous neighbours, limiting the coordination benefit of the communication channel. The FGSv3-PPO advantage over CoLight is 68\% on ingolstadt21 (68.4~s vs.\ 216.8~s mean delay) and $\text{FGS}_\text{S}$ outperforms CoLight by 22\% on cologne8 (22.2~s vs.\ 28.6~s). Notably, CoLight underperforms even independent DQN on every German scenario tested, suggesting that its static attention mechanism does not transfer well from the regular Chinese grid networks on which it was validated.

\paragraph{FGS versus independent DQN and PPO.}
On the largest Regional scenario, FGSv3-PPO outperforms both DQN (78.7~s) and PPO (85.0~s) in mean delay. At Corridor scale, independent PPO is competitive with or better than $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$: on ingolstadt7, PPO achieves 24.1~s versus $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$'s 26.1~s. This is consistent with the scale-dependency argument: at $N=7$, the local observation horizon is sufficient for PPO's on-policy updates to converge to effective policies within 200~episodes, and graph communication adds coordination overhead without sufficient benefit at this scale. At $N=21$, the multi-hop dependency structure is large enough that FGS's explicit spatial coordination produces a material advantage. The result for first-generation $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ on ingolstadt21 (234.9~s) reflects the centralized-critic computational bottleneck rather than a limit of graph-aware coordination; the architectural fix in FGSv3 closes this gap (Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_DesignEvolution}).

\paragraph{FGS versus standalone FRAP.}
Standalone FRAP without graph communication or RL achieves 29.8~s on ingolstadt7 and 40.1~s on cologne8 --- considerably worse than FGS variants on the same scenarios and worse than CoLight on cologne8 (28.6~s). FRAP's phase-competition prior operates without inter-agent coordination, and its encoding assumptions produce a structural mismatch on German intersection topologies that amplifies with scale, as shown in Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_ComponentAnalysis}.

Figure~\ref{fig:radar_comparison} provides a multi-metric comparison for the Regional-tier scenarios, confirming that mean delay rankings are consistent across all four evaluation metrics.

\subsection{Encoder type: FRAP versus MLP}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_EncoderAnalysis}

FRAP~\citep{zheng2019learning} constructs phase-pair competition features $\mathbf{c}_{i,a,b}$ under the assumption that each phase controls a fixed pair of opposing movements. German RESCO intersections violate this assumption: cologne8 and ingolstadt21 mix junctions with 2, 4, and 6 phases, each controlling a variable number of movements. When a phase controls fewer than two movements, the competition weight normalization $\min(\sum P, 2)/\max(\sum P, 1)$ in FRAP's phase-pair embedding falls below the nominal value, producing undersaturated features that the downstream actor misinterprets as low-demand signals. In the extreme case of a single-movement phase (e.g.\ a pedestrian crossing or a turn-only lane), the FRAP output approaches zero --- not because demand is low, but because the encoding is structurally inapplicable. The actor therefore selects phases that satisfy FRAP's encoding assumption rather than actual traffic demand.

The ablation quantifies this mismatch directly. On Cologne Regional the mean delay penalty is 11\% (FRAP+GATv2+PPO: 24.6~s vs.\ MLP+GATv2+PPO: 24.0~s); on Ingolstadt Regional it is $2.6\times$ (617.0~s vs.\ 234.9~s). The scaling of the penalty with network size --- from moderate at $N=8$ to catastrophic at $N=21$ --- confirms that FRAP's structural mismatch amplifies as the number of heterogeneous-phase junctions increases. Figures~\ref{fig:ablation_encoder} and~\ref{fig:frap_vs_original_ingolstadt21} show the corresponding training trajectories: MLP+GATv2+PPO converges stably on both scenarios, while FRAP+GATv2+PPO diverges on ingolstadt21 and maintains persistently high queue lengths throughout training.

Near-competitive FRAP performance on cologne8 ($+11\%$) --- despite FRAP's structural mismatch --- suggests that GATv2 attention can partially compensate by concentrating weight on neighbours with more reliable encodings. This compensation is overwhelmed at $N=21$, where the proportion of FRAP-misaligned junctions is larger and corrupted features propagate through multiple GATv2 hops before reaching the actor. This finding is of independent interest to the MARL-TSC community: FRAP has been widely adopted in published methods~\citep{zheng2019learning,wei2019colight} without topology-specific validation, and the results here establish that its inductive bias does not transfer uniformly to networks with heterogeneous phase structures.

\subsection{Graph-attention mechanism: GATv2 versus GAT}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_GraphAnalysis}

Replacing GATv2 with the original dot-product GAT on Ingolstadt Regional inflates mean delay from 234.9~s to 492.0~s --- a $2.1\times$ increase. On Cologne Regional the difference is modest (24.0~s vs.\ 24.9~s, 4\%). GATv2's expressiveness advantage lies in its per-edge dynamic attention: the coefficient $e_{ij} = \mathbf{a}^\top \operatorname{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{W}[\mathbf{h}_i \| \mathbf{h}_j])$ is a function of both source and destination node features, enabling structurally distinct nodes to receive different attention weights even when their raw features are similar~\citep{brody2022how}. In ingolstadt21's irregular topology, each intersection has a unique demand--phase profile; dynamic attention exploits this heterogeneity to focus each agent's aggregation on the most informative neighbours. Dot-product GAT assigns nearly identical attention to nodes sharing feature-space proximity regardless of their neighbourhood context, producing averaging rather than selective aggregation. On cologne8's smaller and more homogeneous topology, both mechanisms converge to similar solutions, consistent with the expectation that dynamic distinguishability is most valuable where neighbourhood contexts are highly heterogeneous.

\subsection{RL algorithm: SAC versus PPO}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_RLAnalysis}

Two findings emerge from the RL algorithm ablation. First, on Cologne Regional, SAC achieves lower mean delay than PPO with the same encoder and graph layer (22.2~s vs.\ 24.0~s, an 8\% improvement), consistent with SAC's off-policy sample efficiency: the prioritized experience replay buffer reuses past transitions more effectively than PPO's on-policy rollouts within the same 200-episode budget. Second, first-generation SAC fails entirely on Ingolstadt Regional: the centralized critic input dimension of approximately 3{,}133 at $N=21$ exhausts GPU memory after 17~episodes (Appendix~\ref{sec:Appendix_critic_complexity}), while PPO's per-agent value function input remains $O(d)$ independent of $N$.

\paragraph{Reward specification gaming.}
Figure~\ref{fig:reward_gaming} illustrates a failure mode specific to early-training SAC checkpoints on Corridor scenarios. The best-validation checkpoint for $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ on cologne3 occurs at episode~5, where mean delay is anomalously low but mean queue length reaches 46.1~veh./lane --- physically incompatible with genuine low delay in any steady state. The mechanism is \emph{reward specification gaming}~\citep{skalse2022defining}: SUMO defines a vehicle's waiting time as accumulated time stopped; the counter pauses the instant the vehicle starts moving, regardless of whether it clears the stop line. Rapid phase cycling releases one lane briefly per phase switch, collecting a waiting-time-reduction spike, while vehicles in all other lanes continue to queue. SAC's entropy bonus $\alpha\,\mathcal{H}(\pi)$ explicitly rewards distributing probability across phases, giving phase cycling a double incentive: both the traffic reward and the entropy bonus increase simultaneously. PPO avoids this pathway because it has no explicit entropy term in the reward and discards stale transitions after each episode, directly observing the resulting queue inflation in the next update. All checkpoints with physically inconsistent queue--delay combinations are marked~$\ddagger$ in the tables and excluded from the Bold convention. Replacing the differential waiting-time reward with a pressure or queue-clearing signal~\citep{wei2019presslight} is a direct recommendation for future work.

\paragraph{Factored critic and SAC scalability.}
The FGS final design replaces the centralized critic with a factored neighbourhood critic (Section~\ref{sec:Methodology_PipelineArchitecture}) whose input dimension is $O(d_c + A_{\max}) = 204$ at ingolstadt21 --- a $15\times$ reduction from the centralized critic (Appendix~\ref{sec:Appendix_fgsv3_critic_complexity}). This enables both FGSv2-SAC and FGSv3-SAC to complete training at $N=21$. The factored critic achieves effective neighbourhood information pooling by operating on GATv2-enriched local embeddings rather than raw joint observations, so the critic input remains tractable at any $N$ while still incorporating multi-hop demand signals via the communication layer.

\paragraph{Cologne Regional ($N=8$).}
$\text{FGS}_\text{S}$(MLP+GATv2+SAC) achieves stable convergence on cologne8, stabilising near 22--24~s mean delay between episodes~22 and~35. FGSv3-PPO is the most stable run on this scenario, reaching its best checkpoint (21.6~s delay) at episode~39 with minimal variance. The relatively small differences between FGS variants on cologne8 (22.2--24.9~s mean delay) reflect the moderate topology: at $N=8$ all learning methods converge to competitive policies within 200~episodes, and differences are driven by algorithm-level sample efficiency rather than coordination architecture.

\paragraph{Ingolstadt Regional ($N=21$).}
FGSv3-PPO is the only FGS variant with stable, convergent behaviour on ingolstadt21: the validation curve stabilises in the 68--85~s mean delay range from episode~7, with the best checkpoint at 68.4~s (episode~90). $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$(MLP+GATv2+PPO) completes all 200~episodes but shows a large train-to-validation gap (training stable at $\approx$78~s; best-validation 234.9~s), suggesting overfitting to the training demand distribution. FGSv2-SAC and FGSv3-SAC exhibit high-variance trajectories: best checkpoints occur at early training episodes (episode~3 and episode~1 respectively), and the stable post-episode-6 median is approximately 53--55~s for both SAC variants.

\paragraph{Corridor tier.}
Figure~\ref{fig:training_curves_corridor} shows that at Corridor scale all learning methods converge to similar mean delay ranges within 100~episodes, with PPO achieving the lowest final delay on both scenarios. There are no persistent multi-episode spikes, recovery from transient demand surges is fast, and variance between methods is smaller than at Regional scale. This is consistent with the scale-dependency argument: at $N \leq 7$, local observations are sufficient for independent policies to converge effectively within the available training budget.

\section{Design Evolution}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_DesignEvolution}
%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The FGS pipeline was developed through three successive generations, each motivated by a specific module-interface failure exposed during evaluation of the preceding design. This iterative diagnosis-and-repair cycle is itself a methodological contribution: the three-module pipeline structure provides enough isolation between components to identify, localise, and correct individual failure modes without redesigning the whole framework. Table~\ref{tab:fgs_generation_summary} summarises the trajectory; Figure~\ref{fig:fgs_generation_improvement} shows the performance improvement on Ingolstadt Regional.

Gen-1 exposed two failures: (1)~FRAP's scalar output discards per-phase competition information before graph aggregation; (2)~the centralized critic's $O(Nd)$ input prevents SAC from completing training at $N=21$. FGSv2 fixed failure~(1) by tapping FRAP before scalar projection, preserving per-phase action tokens $\mathbf{z}_{i,a}$ through the graph aggregation step. Evaluation of FGSv2 then revealed a third failure: the GATv2 message was a pooled competition score encoding phase rankings rather than demand state --- a suboptimal signal when agents need to anticipate upstream pressure. The FGS final design fixed both remaining failures simultaneously: the Module-II signal was replaced with demand embeddings $\mathbf{d}_i$, and the centralized critic was replaced with a factored neighbourhood critic independent of $N$. Because these two changes were introduced together, their individual contributions to the observed 71\% mean delay reduction (Gen-1 $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$: 234.9~s; FGSv3-PPO: 68.4~s on ingolstadt21) cannot be independently decomposed from the current data; a future partial ablation isolating each change is a natural follow-on experiment. Full algorithmic details for each generation are provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:Appendix_EarlierAlgorithms}.

\section{Answering the Research Questions}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_ResearchQuestions}
%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\noindent\textbf{SQ1} \textit{(Baseline comparison):} The FGS pipeline is competitive with or superior to all seven baselines at Regional scale. FGSv3-PPO achieves 68.4~s mean delay on ingolstadt21, outperforming DQN ($-13\%$, 78.7~s), PPO ($-19\%$, 85.0~s), and CoLight ($-68\%$, 216.8~s). At Corridor scale, $\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ outperforms DQN, FRAP, and CoLight on ingolstadt7 but trails independent PPO (26.1~s vs.\ 24.1~s). At Single-intersection scale, FGS provides no consistent improvement. The competitive envelope grows with network scale, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:scale_benefit}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{SQ2} \textit{(Design-space analysis):} Encoder choice is the dominant local determinant: FRAP-based encoders produce mean delay 11\% to $2.6\times$ higher than MLP encoders, with the penalty scaling with network size due to structural mismatch with German intersection phase heterogeneity. Graph-attention type governs policy generalization at Regional scale: GATv2 reduces mean delay by $2.1\times$ compared with GAT on ingolstadt21, with negligible difference on cologne8. RL algorithm choice is scenario-dependent: SAC is more sample-efficient at $N \leq 8$ but requires a factored critic architecture to complete training at $N=21$. Full component-level analysis in Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_ComponentAnalysis}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{SQ3} \textit{(Scale and topology dependency):} The graph-communication benefit is negligible at $N=1$ (structural no-op), emerging at Corridor scale ($N=7$: FGS beats DQN, FRAP, CoLight but trails PPO), and dominant at Regional scale ($N=21$: FGSv3-PPO leads all baselines). GATv2 becomes critical at $N=21$: substituting GAT doubles mean delay. Encoder mismatch amplifies with scale. The scale threshold for consistent graph-communication advantage is approximately $N=8$--$21$ for the German RESCO topologies evaluated, grounded in the spatio-temporal dependency argument of Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_ScaleDependency} (see Figure~\ref{fig:scale_benefit}).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{SQ4} \textit{(Exploratory --- decentralised self-organisation):} Training dynamics on ingolstadt21 provide indirect evidence of coordinated behaviour: isolated delay spikes are followed by rapid multi-episode recovery across multiple agents, a pattern consistent with GATv2-relayed demand signals propagating corrective information across the network. Without logged attention weights or explicit inter-agent coordination metrics, causal attribution is not possible. SQ4 is treated as a discussion lens rather than a formally answerable question; full attention-weight visualisation and inter-agent coordination analysis are deferred to future work (Section~\ref{sec:ConclusionFutureWork_FutureResearch}).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Main Research Question:} The FGS pipeline is competitive and scalable at Regional scale. FGSv3-PPO outperforms all seven baselines on the largest evaluated scenario (ingolstadt21) and all graph-based baselines at every tested tier. Scalability is achieved through the three-generation diagnosis-and-repair trajectory, which identifies and corrects three distinct module-interface failures. The primary design determinants, in order of impact, are: (1)~encoder structural compatibility with the target topology; (2)~attention mechanism expressiveness at scale; and (3)~critic architecture scalability for the RL algorithm.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_Limitations}
%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Single-seed results.}
All performance figures correspond to single training runs (seed~0). The RESCO benchmark protocol targets five independent seeds with result averaging and standard-deviation reporting; resource constraints precluded this within the available training budget. Performance figures are point estimates without variance bounds and should be interpreted as indicative rather than statistically conclusive. Multi-seed completion over seeds $\{0,1,2,3,4\}$ with bootstrap confidence intervals constitutes the highest-priority follow-on validation.

\paragraph{Training instability of SAC variants on ingolstadt21.}
FGSv2-SAC and FGSv3-SAC complete training at $N=21$ with the factored critic but exhibit high-variance trajectories without clear convergence within 100~episodes. Their best-checkpoint values (FGSv3-SAC: 45.3~s delay at validation episode~3; FGSv2-SAC: 53.7~s at episode~1) are early-training transients driven by entropy-bonus exploration, not evidence of reliable policy quality. This behaviour is consistent with two compounding structural factors: the \emph{deadly triad} of off-policy experience replay, function approximation, and bootstrapped targets identified as a source of instability in deep RL~\citep{van2018deep}; and MARL non-stationarity, where stored transitions become progressively off-distribution as all 21 agents co-evolve~\citep{henderson2018deep}. Stable, converged SAC performance on ingolstadt21 would likely require 300--500 training episodes with entropy-temperature tuning calibrated to the 21-agent joint action space.

\paragraph{Reward specification gaming.}
Early-training SAC checkpoints on Corridor scenarios exhibit physically inconsistent queue--delay combinations (Figure~\ref{fig:reward_gaming} and Section~\ref{sec:ResultsAndDiscussion_RLAnalysis}). The root cause is the differential waiting-time reward: a proxy signal reducible by rapid phase cycling without clearing queues~\citep{skalse2022defining}. All affected checkpoints are marked~$\ddagger$ and excluded from comparisons. As noted above, replacing this reward with a pressure or queue-clearing signal~\citep{wei2019presslight} addresses the root cause and is a direct recommendation for future work.

\paragraph{Train-to-validation demand shift.}
$\text{FGS}_\text{P}$ shows a substantially larger train-to-validation gap on ingolstadt21 than DQN (training delay $\approx$78~s for both; best-validation 234.9~s for FGS vs.\ 78.7~s for DQN), suggesting that the attention-conditioned representation is more sensitive to demand distribution shift between training and validation seeds. FGSv3-PPO reduces this gap (stable validation from episode~7). Domain randomisation during training and multi-seed averaging are the natural remedies and constitute priority directions for future work alongside multi-seed statistical validation.
